# PATTERN 7 — Greed / Holding Too Long

---

> **THE LAW**
> *Greed isn't wanting too much. It's the Speculator renegotiating the plan mid-trade — at the moment when he has the most leverage.*

---

## THE HOOK

The trade hit your target. Or it was close — close enough that the Speculator made a case for letting it continue. "The momentum is strong. The structure supports continuation. Comparable moves have gone further." You didn't take the target. You let it run.

Price stalled. Then reversed. Then you started bargaining: "I'll exit at breakeven." Then it hit your stop. A 2R winner became a loss.

Or less dramatically: you took a smaller gain than planned, repeatedly, because "it looked like it might reverse." Over a month, the aggregate gap between your targets and your actual exits is substantial — but it happened one small concession at a time.

**The Speculator's timing is not random.** He speaks loudest when the trade is working, when his argument is most plausible, when the emotional momentum of being right inflates every reason to stay. That's not coincidence. That's leverage.

---

## THE HUG

**What the impulse is protecting:** The fear of leaving behind what the market offered. This operates at the identity layer: "a good trader would have captured that extended move." Missing the continuation doesn't just cost money — it feels like evidence of inadequacy. Leaving value behind is a story about not being capable enough to extract what was available.

This identity component explains why the specific pain of watching a trade reverse after the missed target is so acute: it's not just the financial cost. It's both mistakes in one — failed to take the target and then lost it all.

---

## THE UNCERTAINTY BEHIND THE HUG

**The concrete question the urgency is asking:**
*Does "letting it run" past the planned target actually produce better outcomes than taking the target — over a meaningful sample?*

The Speculator's argument for each individual trade is contextually reasonable. The contextual reasoning is exactly the problem: it's cherry-picked at peak optimism. The question needs to be asked across 20 trades, not in the moment of one.

---

## THE MECHANISM

### The Mid-Trade Renegotiation

The Speculator's argument for abandoning the target arrives at the worst possible moment: when the trade is in profit and confirmation bias is strongest. The trader is positively activated. Every piece of market data is being interpreted through the lens of "this is working." The Strategist's pre-trade reasoning — "expected value was calculated based on this exit" — is abstract and backward-looking. The Speculator's reasoning is vivid and present.

The Strategist has one disadvantage in this negotiation: his best argument (the pre-trade expected value calculation) was made 45 minutes ago. The Speculator's best argument (the current momentum) is happening right now. In an in-trade negotiation, recency wins.

The solution is to move the negotiation to before the trade — where the Strategist has the field advantage.

---

### Transgression — The Extended Plan

A trader's target is at resistance 2.2R away. The trade reaches it. The Speculator: "look at that momentum — this is going through resistance, not stopping." The trader holds.

Price stalls at resistance. Retraces. He waits for a pullback. It continues. He bargains: "I'll exit at breakeven." Hits his stop. A 2.2R winner is a 1R loser.

He does not know, from his own data, whether letting it run past his target produces better outcomes than taking it. The Speculator has been making this argument for months. It has never been formally tested.

---

### Observance — The Pre-Authorised Runner

Same trader, redesigned. Before entry: 75% of the position exits at the defined target — non-negotiable, pre-committed. 25% is designated as the runner, with a trailing stop rule defined before the trade. The Speculator gets his piece. He doesn't get to renegotiate the 75%.

At target: the primary exits. The runner continues. If momentum carries further, the runner captures it. If price reverses immediately, the runner exits at the trailing stop.

Over 20 trades: the primary target provides consistent R:R. The runner sometimes adds material gain. The Speculator gets participation. The Strategist's edge is preserved for the majority of the position.

---

## THE ARCHITECTURE

"Letting it run" without defined rules is not trend-following. It's Speculator-managed position management — which means holding until the loss aversion of giving back profits forces an exit. That exit is almost always later and worse than a rule-based exit would have been, because the Speculator's exit criterion is "when it hurts enough to stop," not "when the structure changes."

The runner design is the ESS solution: a defined piece with defined rules, non-expandable, agreed before emotional activation. The runner allocation must be small enough that losing it entirely doesn't materially damage the session — otherwise the Speculator will renegotiate the runner too.

## ESS DESIGN

**Short-term (pirate fee):** Pre-define the runner allocation (typically 20–25% of position) and its trailing stop rule before every trade. The Speculator gets a defined channel. The primary position (75–80%) exits at the pre-defined target — no negotiation, no "this time is different." The runner's trailing stop must also be defined before the trade, not improvised during it.

**Long-term (the cure):** After 20 trades tracking "took target" vs. "held past target" separately, the comparison shows what letting it run actually costs or contributes in R terms. If the runner adds positive expectancy — great, it's part of the plan. If it consistently gives back more than it gains — the Speculator's argument has been answered with data, and the full-target exit becomes Stage 2 conviction.

---

## THE EXPERIMENT

For the next 20 trades that reach the original target: log what you actually did and what the trade did in the 30 minutes after your target was reached. After 20: how often did holding past the target produce a better outcome? How often did the runner (if pre-defined) outperform just taking the target? Does the runner add positive expectancy at its current allocation? That comparison defines whether letting it run is adding edge or giving it back.
