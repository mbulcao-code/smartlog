# PATTERN 4 — Revenge Trading

---

> **THE LAW**
> *Revenge trading isn't irrational. It's the most rational response to an injustice — and that's exactly why it's so hard to stop.*

---

## THE HOOK

The stop was hit. The setup was correct — structurally sound, all conditions met. Price reversed immediately after, ran exactly where you said it would, without you in the trade. The loss was clean. The result was infuriating.

Within minutes, you were looking for something else to trade. Not because a new setup had formed. Because something needed to be corrected.

**This isn't a failure of composure.** The injustice signal is legitimate — something was taken, and the instinct to fight back, restore the balance, not be a passive victim is built into the deepest goal layer humans have. The problem isn't the instinct. The problem is that the market is the wrong arena to pursue it in — and the next trade, taken under that activation, is structurally worse than any other trade you'll take all week.

---

## THE HUG

**What the impulse is protecting:** Agency and fairness. The loss produced a specific combination: financial loss plus the acute injustice of being right about the direction and wrong on the trade. The HUG wants to restore both — the money, and the story of competence. A winning trade is the only available response that addresses both at once.

The revenge trade also doesn't need to be profitable to partially succeed. The act of taking it delivers some of the agency the HUG is seeking. This is why the behavior persists even when the pattern of losses is documented: the HUG is satisfied by the action, not only by the outcome.

---

## THE UNCERTAINTY BEHIND THE HUG

**The concrete question the urgency is asking:**
*Do trades taken within 30 minutes of a loss underperform cold-start trades — and does the account recover faster through good setups or through immediate re-entry?*

The belief driving the revenge trade is "recovery requires immediate action." This belief has never been tested against data. If post-loss trades systematically underperform, the belief is wrong — and the urgency that generated it is accelerating the damage rather than repairing it.

---

## THE MECHANISM

### The Injustice Response

Losses don't just produce financial pain — they activate a fairness response. The loss felt unearned: the analysis was correct, the structure was right, the outcome was wrong. The injustice signal demands an active response. The market is the available arena. The revenge trade is the available response.

The Critic arrives simultaneously: "you made a mistake that needs correcting." This frame matters — it recruits the Speculator ("this trade can fix it") and they together override the Strategist ("the next setup isn't here yet"). The congress operates under distorted activation. The decision quality is structurally impaired.

What makes the post-loss window specifically dangerous: loss aversion is at peak activation, the Critic is loudest, regulatory resources are partially depleted from processing the loss, and any setup that "looks like" an opportunity gets evaluated under these conditions. The selection criteria loosen without the trader noticing.

---

### Transgression — The Corrective Trade

A trader's stop is hit on a clean setup. $280 loss. Price reverses and runs to target without him. He experiences financial loss plus acute directional injustice.

Within 12 minutes, he enters a new position — not a defined setup, a directional read on a correlated instrument that "looks similar." He sizes up slightly. He needs to recover the $280.

The trade fails. He's now down $620. The injustice signal intensifies. He enters again. By session end he's down $1,400. The original $280 loss was recoverable in 2 good sessions. The revenge sequence turned it into his worst month.

---

### Observance — The Injustice Protocol

Same trader, redesigned. He defines the post-loss procedure before any session:

After any stop hit: close the platform for 20 minutes. During those 20 minutes: write the setup conditions on a card, confirm all three conditions were genuinely present, verify the stop placement was structurally correct. If all three: mark in the log — *valid trade with a valid outcome*. This is not a loss requiring correction. It is one instance of the expected failure rate.

The card exercise is active, market-adjacent, and has a defined completion point. It delivers the agency the HUG needs without opening a new trade. After 20 minutes: only trades from the defined setup list, only if conditions are met. No exceptions for recovery.

Over 30 sessions, he compares post-loss trades (taken outside the protocol) to cold trades. The data shows: post-loss trades win at 34%, cold trades at 67%. The protocol is not just a rule — it's a response to a comparison he generated himself.

---

## THE ARCHITECTURE

The 30-minute post-loss window is structurally different from any other period in the session. Loss aversion peaks immediately after a loss and decays over 20–40 minutes. The Critic is most active, the Strategist most depleted, the Speculator most motivated by recovery framing. Trade selection under these conditions is expected-value negative — not occasionally, systematically.

The replacement behaviour for revenge trading must meet three criteria: active (passive waiting leaves the injustice signal unresolved), trading-adjacent (satisfies the need to respond without opening a trade), and procedurally closed (has a defined endpoint). The card exercise meets all three. So does a structured next-setup identification — actively scanning for the next clean entry and marking it on watch. Active, relevant, no trade taken.

## ESS DESIGN

**Short-term (pirate fee):** The post-loss protocol channels the agency need without the trade. Define it before any session. Execute it every time — not when you feel like it, every time. The Critic's frame ("you made a mistake requiring correction") is challenged explicitly by the card review. The Speculator's urgency is redirected into setup identification.

**Long-term (the cure):** Log every post-loss trade as "post-loss" category. After 20–30 instances, compare win rate and R:R to cold trades. The comparison almost always shows material underperformance. That number — from your own sessions — is the empirical case for the protocol. Not a rule about discipline. A comparison that answers the question the urgency was raising.

---

## THE EXPERIMENT

For the next 30 sessions: define your maximum daily loss in R (e.g. 2R). Log every trade taken within 30 minutes of a stop hit as "post-loss." After 30 sessions: compare the win rate and average R of post-loss trades to cold trades. What is the average additional loss generated by the revenge sequence on the days it occurred? That number is the monthly cost of the pattern.
