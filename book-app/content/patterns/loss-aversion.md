# PATTERN 10 — Loss Aversion

---

> **THE LAW**
> *Loss aversion isn't a flaw in your thinking. It's an evolved threat response running in an environment it wasn't built for — and it performs exactly as designed, which is exactly the problem.*

---

## THE HOOK

Your stop is approaching. Not there yet — but close enough that each tick feels amplified. The Protector runs the argument: "it might come back. The thesis is still intact. Give it a chance." You move the stop, or you hold without a defined exit, waiting to see. Price doesn't come back. The loss is larger than planned, with more emotional cost on top.

**You didn't fail at discipline.** The evolved survival system that kept your ancestors alive by treating resource loss as a potential death event fired at a trading loss with the same intensity. It performs exactly as designed. The problem is that trading losses are not existential threats — but the system doesn't know that.

The goal isn't to stop feeling loss aversion. That is not possible and not necessary. The goal is to build a system that functions correctly in its presence.

---

## THE HUG

**What the impulse is protecting:** The story of being right, not the capital. Taking the stop confirms the thesis was wrong. As long as the position is open, the thesis is unresolved — which means being right remains possible. The financial loss is acceptable at the time of entry (the stop was set with full knowledge). What becomes unacceptable as price approaches is the confirmation of being wrong.

---

## THE UNCERTAINTY BEHIND THE HUG

**The concrete question the urgency is asking:**
*How often does price actually recover after reaching my stop level — and what does holding past the stop cost compared to taking it, over a meaningful sample?*

The Protector's "it might come back" is trivially true. Some trades do recover. The question is how often, compared to how often they don't, and what the aggregate cost of holding is versus the aggregate cost of taking the stop. This comparison needs counted data.

---

## THE MECHANISM

### The Stop as WHEN

The stop is the most important WHEN in the trade structure. It answers: *how bad can this get, and when is this definitively over?* A defined stop closes this loop. An undefined or renegotiated stop reopens it — worse than before, because the mind now doesn't know where the actual boundary is.

Loss aversion is not static — it peaks immediately as the stop level approaches and as price moves closer. This creates the specific vulnerability: the urgency is highest exactly when the structural decision is most critical. System 1 is at maximum activation at the precise moment the Strategist needs to execute.

---

### Transgression — The Kept Possibility

A trader's stop is at 1R below entry at structural level. Price approaches. Instead of executing, he reviews: "this level might hold." He moves the stop 0.5R lower. Price reaches the original stop level, consolidates for two bars, then breaks down decisively. He's now 1.5R against entry. "I'll wait for a bounce." The bounce comes to 1.2R against. He holds. Price breaks again to 2.1R against. He exits. A planned 1R loss became 2.1R — plus 40 minutes of regulatory depletion that degraded the next two decisions.

---

### Observance — The Structural Commitment

Same trader, different system. He places the stop order at the structural level at entry — not as a mental note, as a live working order. He defines: "when price reaches this level, I exit on the next available bar. There is no evaluation at the stop level."

Trade moves against him. Price reaches the stop. He exits. 1R loss.

Price consolidates at that level, then breaks down further. He watches from flat — with a 1R loss, not a 2.1R loss, and without the regulatory cost of 40 minutes of "should I get out?" His attention is available for the next setup.

---

## THE ARCHITECTURE

Loss aversion has two amplifiers that are specific to trading:

**Proximity amplification:** the emotional weight of a potential loss increases as it becomes more imminent and certain. The Protector's urgency at "price 0.2R from stop" is substantially higher than the same loss calculated abstractly before the trade. This is why the stop that felt comfortable before entry feels intolerable when price approaches it.

**Asymmetric memory:** recovered positions (where holding past the stop worked) are emotionally salient and memorable. Stopped positions that continued lower are filed as expected outcomes. The Protector's sample is biased toward the memorable recoveries.

The log corrects both: it creates an objective record of what happened after each stop was hit or held past, removing the memory bias and providing the actual comparison.

## ESS DESIGN

**Short-term (pirate fee):** Place stop orders as live working orders at entry wherever the platform permits. Active cancellation of a pre-set stop is a much higher bar than failing to manually execute a mental stop. The Protector must override an already-committed decision rather than prevent one from being made.

**Long-term (the cure):** After 20 instances of logging what happened to price in the 30 minutes after a stop was hit, the data provides the actual recovery rate. The Protector's "it might come back" now competes with a number — not "most of the time" or "sometimes," but an actual percentage from actual trades.

---

## THE EXPERIMENT

For the next 20 trades: after any stop is hit, log what the trade did in the 30 minutes following the stop. How often did price reverse and recover to your entry? How often did it continue in the loss direction? After 20: what is the actual post-stop recovery rate? That percentage is the data-based counter-argument to "it might come back" — stated as a number the Protector can process.
