# PATTERN 3 — Early Exit / Cutting Profits Short

---

> **THE LAW**
> *Early exits aren't about protecting profits. They're about relief — and relief is the most immediate reward the market offers.*

---

## THE HOOK

The trade was working. It reached 1R, maybe 1.5R. Then price printed a bar with a longer wick, or stalled for two candles, or you started calculating how much you'd give back if it reversed. You exited. The trade continued to your original target without you.

You call it "active trade management" or "taking profits when available." Both frames protect the same thing: the relief you felt the moment the position closed. The relief was real. The framing was cover.

**This isn't a failure of conviction.** It's the mind doing exactly what it was designed to do: terminate an uncomfortable state by the fastest available means. The position in profit is cognitively expensive — the possibility of giving it back exists on every tick. The exit terminates that possibility completely. The relief is immediate and certain. The target profit was delayed and uncertain. Certainty beats probability in the moment every time.

---

## THE HUG

**What the impulse is protecting:** The Protector's goal — secure what you have before it evaporates. The trade in profit is not a win yet. It's a potential win that could become a loss. The exit converts potential into realised. That conversion is the reward — not the amount, but the termination of uncertainty.

For some traders there's also an identity component: "I am a trader who takes profits, not one who watches them disappear."

---

## THE UNCERTAINTY BEHIND THE HUG

**The concrete question the urgency is asking:**
*Does holding to my original target actually produce better outcomes than my typical early exit — over a meaningful sample?*

The Protector argues "it might reverse" with genuine conviction, because it sometimes does. Without comparison data, the trader has no counter-argument with statistical weight. The Protector needs a number, not a principle.

---

## THE MECHANISM

### The Relief Loop

The early exit habit runs on a clean, fast reward loop:

Position in profit and moving → comfort.
Price stalls or retraces slightly → discomfort (possibility of giving back gains).
Early exit → immediate relief (uncertainty terminated, gain locked).
Reward: discomfort gone.

The loop is powerful because the relief is **immediate and certain**. The profit from holding to target is **delayed and uncertain**. The brain's reward system doesn't calculate expected value in real time — it weights certainty and immediacy heavily against probability and delay. The early exit wins this calculation structurally, not because the trader is weak.

Adding to the problem: the trade's original target was almost certainly committed loosely — known, but not written down, not structurally pre-committed. The Protector can renegotiate any target that exists only as an intention.

---

### Transgression — The Managed Exit

A trader enters long, targets 2.6R. The trade moves cleanly to 1.3R. A bar forms with an extended upper wick. The Protector speaks: *this might be the high — lock it in.*

He exits at 1.3R. Price retraces briefly, holds the structure, pushes to 2.6R without him. He captured exactly half of what was available. He logs it as "active management" and notes how disciplined he was to take profit.

He had no data that early exits underperform holds. The relief the exit delivered was real. The framing that made it feel disciplined was borrowed from a story that protected the habit.

---

### Observance — The Pre-Committed Target

Same trader, different design. Before entering, he writes on a card: *Target: 2.6R. Stop: [structural level]. No exit until one of these is hit.* He places the card visible during the trade.

The trade runs the same path. The wick forms. The Protector speaks. He reads the card.

Now the exit is framed differently: it would override a decision made in cold blood by the pre-session Strategist. Not "active management" — "breaking a decision I made when I was thinking clearly." That framing shift, applied consistently, changes the architecture of the choice.

He holds. Target hits. He didn't feel calm throughout. But the pre-commitment changed which question the exit had to answer.

---

## THE ARCHITECTURE

The early exit is a textbook case of hyperbolic discounting: the cognitive bias where immediate rewards are weighted disproportionately relative to their actual value. The relief (immediate, certain) is processed at full weight. The foregone target (delayed, probabilistic) is processed at discounted weight. The expected-value calculation that says "hold" is running in System 2 — slowly. The relief response is running in System 1 — immediately and at full intensity.

System 1 wins this exchange without structural design. The solution is not to make System 2 faster. It's to pre-load System 2's conclusion into the decision before System 1 activates — which is what the written target and pre-commitment accomplish.

## ESS DESIGN

**Short-term (pirate fee):** Pre-define a partial exit. Before entry, split the position: 50-60% runs to the full target; 40-50% exits at an interim level (1R or halfway to target). The Protector gets his fee — a guaranteed partial win is locked. The remainder runs with conviction. Both portions have defined exit rules established before the trade opens. The pirate is on salary before the trade begins.

**Long-term (the cure):** After 20+ trades tracking "partial/early exit" vs. "held to target" variants separately, the comparison shows what early exits cost in R terms. That number — calculated from the trader's own sessions — is the counter-argument the Protector can actually process. Not "you should hold more." The actual average cost per early exit, in R, from 20 real trades.

---

## THE EXPERIMENT

For the next 20 trades: before entry, write the target price and stop price on a card. Place it visible. When the urge to exit early appears, read the card. Log: did you hold or exit early? If early, what did the trade do after? After 20: calculate the average R difference between your early exits and the original target. That is the monthly cost of the habit — stated as a number, not a principle.
