# II.5 — The Pirates' Dilemma

---

> **THE LAW**
> *Feed your pirates without starving your fishermen.*

---

## LAYER 1 — The Model

When you cannot eliminate the pirate, you can agree on his fee — before the trade begins.

Every trader runs at least three pirates. They represent real, legitimate goals. They're not going away. The traders who look like they have no pirates aren't trading without emotion — they've priced the fee into the plan before the session starts. A known cost is manageable. An unacknowledged one is extracted at will, at the worst possible moment.

**The three pirates:**

**The FOMO Pirate** — wants in. Whether the setup is fully formed or not, the move is happening and he wants to be on it. *Unmanaged:* early entries with full size, regret in both directions (if it loses: "why did I?"; if the level is later reached: "why didn't I wait?"). *Managed short-term:* enter early with reduced size — you're on the trade; the R:R is partially preserved; the HUG is satisfied. Full size reserved for when the full setup is met. *Managed long-term:* early and full-setup entries logged as competing variants. After enough data, the question of whether early entry is worth it gets answered by the numbers, not by the urgency.

**The Protector Pirate** — wants out before it hurts. *Unmanaged:* premature exits, moved stops, the feeling of "something is wrong" overriding the plan. *Managed:* respected stops and moved stops are both logged — how often did the respected stop protect from a worse outcome vs. how often did price reverse after the stop? The data closes the question. Until then: place the stop at the structural level, size the position so the structural stop is the comfortable stop. The Protector's question — *how bad can this get?* — is answered at entry, with a number, before he speaks.

**The Speculator Pirate** — wants more than the plan allows. *Unmanaged:* abandoned targets, "letting it run" with no rule for when running ends, the reversal that erases the profit. *Managed short-term:* a pre-defined runner — a specific portion of the position (e.g., 25%) with a specific trailing rule, agreed-upon before entry. He gets a piece. The piece is defined. He cannot renegotiate during the trade. *Managed long-term:* planned exit and runner logged as competing variants — the data shows what actually maximises expectancy.

**The ESS principle:** Negotiate the fee before the trade. In-trade negotiations always go one direction — the pirate wins. His urgency in the moment is greater than yours. His impatience is already running. Pre-trade, you have the advantage: full cognitive resources, no emotional activation, and the ability to write the terms before you need them.

## LAYER 2 — The Mechanism

### The Unpriced Fee

Every time you don't pre-agree the pirate's fee, he extracts one anyway. The extraction just happens at a time and price of his choosing.

The Speculator's unmanaged fee: a trade is working. He's at 60% of target. *This could go much further.* The trader decides to "let it run" — no revised target, no rule for when to exit, just openness to upside. The trade reverses. He exits at breakeven. He calls it greed. It was an unpriced fee: the Speculator demanded participation in upside that wasn't budgeted for, and extracted it from the original plan's result.

The Protector's unmanaged fee: the same week, a different trade moves against him 0.8R on a 1R stop. *Something is wrong.* He moves the stop. The trade comes back and hits target. He feels relief — his "read" was right. He doesn't see that across 20 similar instances, the moved stop results in a larger loss 14 times. He is paying a fee without seeing the invoice.

The FOMO Pirate's unmanaged fee: the setup triggers at the level. Perfect entry. Then, two hours before the scheduled setup, he sees price approaching the zone early. He enters at half-size "just in case." The level is reached, the full setup triggers, but he's now conflicted — is he adding to a full-size position, or was the early entry the trade? He sizes down. The trade works fully. He made less than he should have — and is now unsure whether the early entry was right or wrong, because he has no data, only a result.

---

### Transgression — Renegotiation During the Trade

A trader with a clean, working position. The Speculator speaks at 70% of target: *this is going to keep running.* He decides to remove the target and trail. No rule for when to trail. No defined trailing instrument. Just: "I'll know when to exit."

Price stalls. The Protector joins: *lock it in.* Now both pirates are speaking simultaneously, with conflicting demands. The Strategist is outvoted. The trader exits at 85% of the original target — neither the planned exit nor the extended version. Both pirates are unsatisfied. The fishermen made less than planned.

The renegotiation happened during the trade, under full emotional activation, with no rules agreed upon beforehand. The result was predictable.

---

### Observance — Pre-Agreed Terms

Same trader, redesigned approach. Before entry, he divides the position explicitly:

*Primary position (75%):* exits at the original target. Non-negotiable. No exceptions during the trade.

*Runner (25%):* follows a defined trailing rule — specifically, trailing by one ATR below the most recent swing high. The Speculator has a piece. The terms are written. He knows what he's getting.

The Protector's terms are already addressed: the stop is placed at the structural level. His question — *how bad can this get?* — has a number. He's satisfied.

When the Speculator speaks at 70% of target, the Strategist's answer is already written: *the 75% exits at target; the runner follows the rule.* There is no renegotiation in progress. The terms were agreed. Both pirates are on salary. Both fishermen are operating. The trade runs to plan.

## LAYER 3 — The Architecture

### The Evolutionarily Stable Strategy

The ESS concept is borrowed from evolutionary game theory (John Maynard Smith, formalised through Richard Dawkins' *The Selfish Gene*). In behavioural biology, an Evolutionarily Stable Strategy is a strategy that, once adopted by a population, cannot be displaced by an alternative — because it's already optimised for the actual conditions of the game, including the behaviour of other players.

The key insight: the *unstable ideal* — the strategy that would be optimal if every player behaved perfectly — is not stable. Because in practice, players don't behave perfectly. And a strategy that assumes perfect behaviour collapses the moment an imperfect player enters the game.

In trading: the ideal strategy is "follow the plan perfectly, feel nothing, execute mechanically." This is the unstable ideal. It collapses the moment the pirate speaks — which is every trade. The strategy that designed for a quiet pirate fails every time the pirate is loud.

An *Emotionally Stable Strategy* is designed for the actual trader — the one who will feel the Protector's urgency and the Speculator's pull. It doesn't assume they're absent. It prices them in. And because it's designed for conditions that actually occur, it holds when those conditions arrive.

---

### ESS Design Principles

**1. Pre-price the fee — in writing, before entry.** Every pirate gets a defined allocation. The Protector gets a stop level that answers his core question. The Speculator gets a runner allocation, if the setup warrants it. The FOMO Pirate gets a defined reduced size for early entries. Everything outside those allocations is not a negotiation — it's a violation of agreed terms.

**2. Make renegotiation structurally impossible.** The most important feature of an ESS is that its terms are not subject to revision during the trade. Not "I'll evaluate when the time comes" — the evaluation happened before entry, in cold blood, with full cognitive resources. Real time is execution. The argument is already over.

**3. Use the pirate's information, not his vote.** The Protector often carries real signal: a structural concern, a condition that wasn't present at entry. An ESS gives him a channel for that information — a pre-defined set of conditions that would justify a plan revision — without giving him the vote. The plan can be updated. But the update requires the Strategist, before the trade, not the Protector, during it.

**4. Design for loud pirates, not quiet ones.** If the ESS only holds when emotions are calm, it's not an ESS. The test of a well-designed strategy is not whether it feels easy on a calm day. It's whether it holds on the day when the Protector is loudest, the streak is worst, and the regulatory resource is most depleted.

---

### Closing Experiment

Identify the pirate who extracts the most expensive fee from you — FOMO (early entries with full size), Protector (moved stops, premature exits), or Speculator (abandoned targets, let-it-run without rules).

For your next 20 trades: design and commit to a pre-agreed fee for that pirate — a specific rule, written before entry, that gives him something real without letting him renegotiate during the trade.

After 20 trades: was the fee worth what it cost? Did the pirate take more or less than the budget? That data tells you whether the terms were right — and how to revise them.
