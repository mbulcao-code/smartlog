# II.4 — The Mind as a Problem-Solver: The 3 Tasks

---

> **THE LAW**
> *Where doubt and alternatives linger, emotions run amok.*

---

## LAYER 1 — The Model

Trading anxiety doesn't come from not knowing what the market will do. It comes from not knowing what *you* will do — and the mind won't rest until it has an answer.

The market has always been uncertain. That's not what changed when the anxiety appeared. What changed is that you entered the trade without giving the mind its three required inputs.

**The 3 Tasks — what the mind needs to find rest:**

| Task | The question | What creates the problem |
|---|---|---|
| **The WHAT** | What is the goal? What am I trying to accomplish? | Unclear or conflicting goals |
| **The HOW** | What is the method? What exactly will I do? | Unclear, untested, or competing methods |
| **The WHAT TO EXPECT** | What does this look like when it's working? What's realistic? | Vague or unrealistic expectations |

When any of the three is incomplete, the mind doesn't accept the ambiguity and move on. It runs a continuous background search — scanning for the signal it was never given. That search *is* the anxiety. You feel it as restlessness, the urge to check prices, the impulse to act on something, anything, just to make the searching stop.

The mind calms down not when the desired outcome arrives — not when the trade works — but when it stops searching for better alternatives. When no decision feels unresolved. When there's nothing left to second-guess. Specificity closes the search. Vagueness keeps it running indefinitely.

## LAYER 2 — The Mechanism

### The Incomplete Problem

The WHAT seems obvious: make money, hit target. But there's a difference between "make money" — which the mind can't evaluate or monitor — and "exit at the previous swing high with 75% of the position" — which the mind can check against reality at any moment.

The HOW also seems obvious: enter when conditions are met. But "conditions are met" is usually far less specific than it sounds. Under pressure, the question *are conditions actually met?* becomes a judgment call. Judgment calls under emotional activation produce inconsistent outputs.

The WHAT TO EXPECT is almost always vague. "This should work" is not an expectation — it's hope. An expectation sounds like: "my setup produces positive outcomes approximately 65% of the time; I should expect to lose roughly one in three, and the current trade may be that one."

When all three are incomplete, the mind runs the search continuously. The search generates pressure. The pressure generates urgency. The urgency generates the behaviour you're trying to stop.

---

### Transgression — The Vague Plan Meets a Live Market

A trader enters a reversal setup. He has a price level. He has a conviction — "this looks like it's turning." What he doesn't have: a defined target price, a specific rule for when the trade is no longer valid, or a clear answer to "what would have to happen for me to be wrong?"

The trade moves in his direction. He feels good. Then price retraces slightly.

Now the search begins: *is this a normal pullback or is this the reversal breaking down?* He doesn't know — because he never defined those two things before entry. They were things he'd "evaluate when the time came."

The time came. The mind has no answer. The Protector speaks. He exits.

The trade then runs to where his original target would have been. He logs it as "hesitation" or "lack of conviction." It was neither. It was an incomplete problem — no WHAT TO EXPECT, no specific HOW for the retrace scenario — and the mind, with no answer, did the only thing it could: stop the search right now by closing the trade.

---

### Observance — The Complete Problem

Same trader, different week. Before entry, he spends three minutes and answers the three questions out loud:

*WHAT:* Exit at the previous swing high. Full position.
*HOW:* Enter on the retest of the broken level. Stop 1.5% below structure. No touching the trade unless stop hits or target hits.
*WHAT TO EXPECT:* This setup produces roughly 60% positive outcomes. The retrace after entry is normal — it's happened in about half my logged instances. A retrace to the 50% level of the entry bar does not change the thesis.

He adds one pre-commitment rule: he will not touch the trade unless one of two events occurs — stop hit, or target hit.

The trade moves in his direction. Price retraces — further than the first scenario, uncomfortably. The Protector speaks. But now the mind has something to do with the feeling: *check the rule. Stop not hit. Target not hit. No action permitted.* The search closes.

He didn't feel calmer because he was more confident in the market. He felt calmer because he had a complete problem — and the mind, given a complete problem, stops searching.

## LAYER 3 — The Architecture

### The Zeigarnik Effect in Trading

In the 1920s, Bluma Zeigarnik observed that people remember interrupted tasks far better than completed ones — because the mind keeps incomplete tasks in active working memory until they're resolved. Every unresolved task generates a continuous low-level cognitive load: a background hum of "this isn't finished yet."

In trading, every vague plan is an open loop. The mind doesn't accept the ambiguity and rest — it monitors the loop continuously. That monitoring shows up as the urge to check prices outside trading hours, the inability to focus on anything else while in a trade, the compulsion to act when price moves even slightly, and the anxiety that feels like it's about the trade but is actually about the unresolved question.

When you define WHAT + HOW + WHAT TO EXPECT before entry, you close the loop at entry — not at exit. The mind's job shifts from *searching for meaning in each tick* to *checking against defined end states.* These are categorically different cognitive loads. The first is exhausting and open-ended. The second is finite and manageable.

A defined stop loss doesn't just protect capital. It converts the ambiguous into the definite: "if price reaches Y, I lose Z, and at that point I exit." The Protector's core question — *how bad can this get?* — is answered before the trade begins. The loop is closed before it can generate pressure.

This is why traders who move their stops feel more anxious afterward, not less. They just converted a defined end state back into an open loop.

---

### Why Vagueness Amplifies Loss Aversion

Kahneman's loss aversion — losses feel roughly twice as significant as equivalent gains — is a baseline figure. But it is amplified substantially by ambiguity. A definite loss of X feels meaningfully less bad than an ambiguous situation where X might happen.

The vague trade is permanent ambiguity. The mind cannot determine whether a given adverse movement is normal fluctuation (tolerate) or a real warning (act). Without the ability to distinguish, it treats everything as a potential warning. Loss aversion runs at full amplification continuously — not because the situation is more dangerous, but because it is undefined.

Specificity is the technical antidote to this amplification. A defined stop level, a defined target, a defined rule for the retrace scenario: each converts a potential ambiguity into a defined decision point. Loss aversion still applies — but it applies at the defined end states, not at every tick.

---

### Confidence as a Structural Output

Most traders believe confidence produces a good plan — that you need to feel ready before you can plan clearly.

The mechanism runs in the opposite direction.

The experience of clarity before a trade — what traders call "conviction" or "readiness" — is the functional output of having closed the three loops. WHAT, HOW, and WHAT TO EXPECT: when all three have answers that the mind can actually check against reality, the mind produces a signal that is experientially indistinguishable from confidence.

Which means confidence isn't something you need to feel before you can execute a plan. It's something the plan produces. The sequence is always: complete the structure first — experience the confidence second. Waiting for confidence before completing the structure is waiting for the output before you've run the process.

---

### Closing Experiment

For 20 trades: before each entry, write down your WHAT (specific target price), HOW (specific entry conditions and one rule for the adverse scenario), and WHAT TO EXPECT (what does a normal adverse movement look like for this setup, and when does it cross into "thesis invalidated?").

After each trade, note: was the WHAT TO EXPECT description accurate? Where did the mind generate the most pressure — and was that pressure covered by your pre-trade answers?

You're not looking for a win rate. You're building the habit of giving the mind a complete problem before asking it to hold a position.
