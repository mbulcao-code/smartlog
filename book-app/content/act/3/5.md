# III.5 — How to Build a Repeatable Setup

---

> **THE LAW**
> *If it doesn't repeat, it's not a setup you can rely on.*

---

## LAYER 1 — The Model

All of Act III depends on one prerequisite: a repeatable situation to test.

Without repeatability, there is no comparison. Without comparison, there is no learning. Without learning, uncertainty never resolves. The most sophisticated logging system, the most careful analysis, the most committed Stage 1 experiment — all of it requires the same thing to produce signal: *the same type of situation occurring enough times to form a meaningful distribution.*

A setup needs four elements to be testable. No more, no less.

---

**Element 1: The Situation**

The context that must be present for the setup to apply. Three components:

- *Type:* Reversal | Breakout | Retest — pick one. A setup that applies to all three is not a setup; it's a general philosophy.
- *Level:* a specific, identifiable price area — prior structure, support/resistance, a key zone. "Near a level" is not specific enough. The level should be identifiable before price reaches it.
- *Direction:* Long or Short. Both directions on the same level is two setups, not one.

---

**Element 2: The Conditions**

The specific criteria that define what must be present for entry — and the variants that capture the uncertainty within each condition.

- Minimum 2 conditions, maximum 5. More conditions reduce the frequency of the setup (fewer instances = slower learning). Each condition that currently generates "but what if…?" is a candidate for a variant — not an additional condition.
- Each condition has variants — the competing alternatives you currently second-guess. Instead of fighting the push to use a different version, you log it.
- Maximum 5 variants per condition (same reasoning as maximum 5 conditions: too many variables makes comparison harder, not more precise).

*Examples of conditions and their variants:*

| Condition | Variant A | Variant B | Variant C |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RSI at entry** | Oversold / Overbought (< 30 / > 70) | Neutral range (30–70) | — |
| **Level strength** | Weak (single touch, no history) | Medium (2–3 touches) | Strong (multiple reactions) |
| **Entry trigger** | At the level (immediate) | Pre-level (X points early) | Post-level / retest confirmation |

Each variant is not a separate setup — it's a question within the setup. Do your entries at the level outperform entries on the retest? Does level strength predict outcome? Log all variants. The data answers the questions.

**The critical rule:** Every condition that generates a "but what if…?" is a candidate for a variant, not an additional condition. The doubt is the test.

---

**Element 3: The Stop**

The price that structurally invalidates the thesis. Not the amount of dollars you can afford to lose — the price that, if reached, proves your entry premise wrong.

- **Structural stop:** placed at the price that proves the thesis wrong — below the structure, below the level zone, at the point where "if price is here, the level failed."
- **Comfort stop:** placed where the loss feels acceptable.

When these two don't match, the fix is not moving the stop to where you're comfortable. The fix is reducing position size until the structural stop becomes the comfortable stop. The stop placement follows the market. The position size follows your risk tolerance.

---

**Element 4: The Targets**

The defined exit — the most likely price to be reached, not the most profitable price to hope for.

- Targets are defined by likelihood, not wishful thinking. "The previous swing high" is a target. "Whatever the move produces" is not.
- When multiple targets: size proportionally to likelihood. More size at the most probable zone.
- Optional runner: a pre-defined portion (e.g. 25%) with a specific trailing rule — agreed before entry, not improvised during.

---

**Minimum viable setup example:**

```
Setup name:     "Key Level Reversal"
Situation:      Price returns to a broken structure level (retest)
Level:          [specific price zone, identifiable pre-entry]
Direction:      Long on bullish reversal evidence at level

Conditions:
  C1: Level reached [Variant A: exact level / B: level ± 5pts / C: level ± 10pts]
  C2: Reversal signal [Variant A: reversal close inside zone / B: first wick, no close]
  C3: Volume [Variant A: below average (absorption) / B: above average]

Stop:       Below the structure — the price at which the level has structurally failed.
            Position size adjusted so this stop = comfortable stop.

Target 1:   Previous swing high — 60% of position (most likely)
Target 2:   Next structure zone — 20% of position
Runner:     Remaining 20%, trailing by one ATR below most recent swing high
```

## LAYER 2 — The Mechanism

### The Setup That Can't Teach You Anything

A trader has been working on a "reversal at levels" approach for six months. He enters when "price comes back to the level and shows a reversal signal." He takes some trades at the exact level, some after a wick, some on a close inside the zone. Sometimes he uses a tight stop just below the wick low; sometimes he uses a wider structural stop. His targets vary based on how the trade "feels."

Every trade is slightly different. He can't separate the results by any meaningful variable. He has 40 trades and no distribution — because there's no consistent definition of what's being traded. Every loss might be because the entry was early, or the stop was too tight, or the level was wrong, or the session timing was off.

The setup exists in concept. It doesn't exist as something testable.

He logs. He reads the logs. He learns nothing from them, because there's nothing to compare.

---

### Observance — The Structured Setup

Same approach, redesigned. He defines:

- The level (identifiable before price arrives, written in advance)
- Two entry trigger variants (at the level vs. on retest confirmation)
- One stop rule (below the level that generated the zone, always structural)
- One target (previous swing high, primary), plus an optional runner with a trailing rule

He now has a testable setup. Every instance is comparable to every other instance, because the variables are defined. When he logs 40 trades across the two entry trigger variants, the comparison is clean: Variant A and Variant B in the same situation, same stop rule, same target. The only thing that differs is the trigger timing.

After 40 trades, his data is clear: retest confirmation (Variant B) wins at 67%; at-level entries (Variant A) win at 44%. The entry trigger question is answered. He retires Variant A. He runs Variant B with conviction.

The same setup, now with learning embedded in the design.

## LAYER 3 — The Architecture

### The Hypothetico-Deductive Method in Trading

Scientific experiments produce learning because they isolate variables. When everything except one thing is held constant, the outcome of changing that one thing is attributable to it — not to the weather, not to the trader's mood, not to a correlated market condition.

A repeatable setup is a trading experiment designed with the same logic. Conditions define what is held constant (the situation, the stop, the target logic). Variants define what is being changed (the entry trigger, the level quality threshold, the timing). The outcome is attributable to the variant because everything else is controlled.

Without this structure, trading data is observational — you're watching outcomes happen without controlling for the variables that produce them. Observational data can suggest patterns. It cannot establish them. The distinction matters because patterns that look real in noisy observational data frequently disappear when properly controlled.

The repeatable setup is the trader's experimental apparatus. Its design determines whether the data it generates is signal or noise.

---

### Why "Maximum 5" Matters

The maximum-5 limit on both conditions and variants is not arbitrary. It's a frequency constraint.

A setup with 5 conditions, each with 3 variants, theoretically produces 3⁵ = 243 distinct combinations. In practice, a trader will only see a handful of those combinations frequently enough to generate data. The others will appear once, twice, never — producing no signal and muddying the averages.

The practical goal: define the setup narrowly enough that the same situation occurs at least 20–30 times in a realistic time frame (weeks to months, depending on the market and timeframe). If the setup occurs once a month, you'll be waiting years for Stage 2. If it occurs 3–4 times a week, you're at Stage 2 in two to three months.

Frequency is a feature, not a bug. More instances per unit time means faster learning.
