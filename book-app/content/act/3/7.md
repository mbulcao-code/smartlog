# III.7 — Logging That Actually Works

---

> **THE LAW**
> *The purpose of logging is not to document history. It's to solve the uncertainty creating the urgency.*

---

## LAYER 1 — The Model

Most trading journals track outcomes. Wins and losses. P&L. "What happened." This produces a pile of data with no comparison. A pile has no distribution. A distribution is what produces learning.

The failure mode is not laziness — it's the wrong question. A journal that asks "what happened?" generates a historical record. A journal that asks "which variant was used, under which conditions, with which outcome?" generates a comparison. And comparisons are the only thing that closes the doubt.

**The true purpose of logging:** transform your doubts into variants to be tested. Let the reports answer the questions. Let the answers dissolve the urgency. Every field in a log entry should earn its presence by answering one of the questions currently costing you the most.

**What to log — and what to skip:**

| Log this | Skip this |
|---|---|
| Which variant was used | How you felt before the trade |
| Whether all conditions were met | General market commentary |
| Entry type (full setup / early / late / random) | Post-hoc analysis of why it worked |
| Exact entry, stop, target at the time of entry | Detailed price action narration |
| The outcome and any deviations from plan | Motivation journaling |

The constraint principle: **log fewer things with more precision.** Three variables logged across 30 trades produces more learning than 15 variables logged across 5 trades.

---

## LAYER 2 — The Full Logging Flow

The logging structure below captures everything needed to run the variant experiments — no more, no less. Each section answers a specific question.

---

**LOG A TRADE**

*Is this a setup trade or a random trade?*
- Setup trade → which setup?
- Random trade → *You can't learn from random trades. Log it anyway — track the damage.*

*How did the setup conditions play out?*
- For each condition: which variant was used?
- Were all conditions met at entry?

---

**TRADE DETAILS**

- Long / Short
- Date (automatic)
- Instrument (optional)
- Entry price / Exit price (optional — useful for R:R tracking)
- Result: **Win / Loss / Breakeven** (required)
- P&L in $ (optional — useful for actual dollar tracking)

---

**HOW WAS YOUR ENTRY?**

This section tracks the entry type — the variant that captures the most expensive source of urgency for most traders.

- **Full setup** — all conditions met at entry
- **Early entry** — FOMO or incomplete setup
  - Full conditions met *after* the entry (the setup confirmed — was the early entry worth it?)
  - Full conditions *not* met after (setup never confirmed — the early entry was a standalone bet)
- **Late entry** — entered after the ideal trigger
- **Hesitation — better price** (waiting produced a better entry)
- **Hesitation — worse price** (waiting cost entry or produced a worse level)
- **Chased into profit** (entered into a running move)
- **Chased into a loss** (entered late into a move that reversed)
- **Random trade** — no predefined setup *(flag clearly: random trades are not comparable to setup trades)*

---

**WHAT BEST DESCRIBES THE TRADE?**

This section tracks the most common sources of outcome deviation.

*Stop:*
- Respected stop
  - Protected from worse loss (price continued further)
  - Price reversed after the stop (painful — but now you have data)
- Changed stop
  - Better result (moving the stop helped)
  - Worse result (moving the stop cost)
- Panic exit — closed manually before stop

*Target:*
- Target not hit
  - Never onside — price went straight against
  - Onside — reversed to breakeven
  - Onside — reversed to a loss
- Early exit
  - Target was hit after the early exit (cost: what did it cost?)
  - Target was not hit after (the early exit protected — log the comparison)
- Multiple targets — X out of Y targets hit
  - Last target hit — optimal exit (prices reached the runner target)
  - Prices kept going — left money on the table
- Delayed exit — worse than the planned exit

---

**ANY OTHER ISSUE?**

Each issue, when selected, reveals a sub-question: was a trusted setup used, or a random trade?

- **Revenge trade** → Used a trusted setup / Random trade (random revenge trades are especially expensive — the data will show this)
- **Overtrading** → Used a trusted setup / Random trade
- **Oversizing** → Used a trusted setup / Random trade
- **Other** → Describe the specific issue

## LAYER 3 — The Architecture

### Why Deviations Matter More Than Outcomes

A trade that follows the plan and loses is informative. A trade that deviates from the plan and wins is not — or at least, not in the way it feels. The win from an unplanned trade is a sample of one from a poorly defined experiment. It tells you very little about whether the deviation was a good decision in expectancy terms.

Tracking deviations separately from plan-adherent trades makes this visible. When you log "respected stop" separately from "changed stop," you can eventually compare: across all instances where I changed the stop, how did outcomes compare to instances where I held it? The comparison requires both populations to be tracked.

Without separate tracking, "I moved the stop and it worked" and "I moved the stop and it didn't work" become indistinguishable noise — and the Protector's urgency keeps citing the memorable times it worked.

---

### The Minimum Viable Log Entry

Designed for the trader who has 30 seconds between trades and a bad memory for detail:

```
Date: [auto]
Setup: [name]
Entry variant: [A / B / C]
Conditions met: [all / partial / none]
Entry: [price]   Stop: [price]   Target: [price]
Result: [win / loss / BE]
Deviation: [none / what happened — one line]
```

That's it. Thirty seconds. Any additional detail is optional enrichment. The minimum is enough to run the comparison that produces learning.
