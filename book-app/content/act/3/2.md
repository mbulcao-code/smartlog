# III.2 — Stage 1: Remove Regret (Run the Experiment)

---

> **THE LAW**
> *You can't remove the uncertainty yet. But you can remove the second-guessing — right now.*

---

## LAYER 1 — The Model

Stage 1 begins with one act: converting a vague feeling into a concrete question.

"I should be more patient" is not a question. It's a verdict — and verdicts can't be tested. "Does entering at full setup conditions outperform early entry?" is a question. It has two measurable alternatives. It can be answered by data. And the moment it becomes a question, the emotional relationship to each instance changes: instead of "did I do the right thing?", the question becomes "did I collect the data point?"

**The four steps of Stage 1:**

**1. Name the uncertainty.** Translate the vague feeling — the guilt, the doubt, the "I should" — into the concrete question it's actually asking.

*Not:* "I should be more patient" → *But:* "Does waiting for the full setup outperform entering on partial confirmation?"
*Not:* "I shouldn't move my stop" → *But:* "Do honoured stops produce better outcomes than moved stops over a sample?"
*Not:* "I always exit too early" → *But:* "Does holding to target outperform my typical exit point over 30 instances?"

**2. Name the competing alternatives.** Every option you currently agonise between becomes a variant. Label them clearly — Variant A and Variant B is enough. The labelling matters because it signals to the mind that both are being studied, not that one is right and the other a failure.

**3. Test — don't repress.** When the impulse fires, don't fight it. Log it. Both variants are part of the experiment. Variant A isn't the "bad" choice — it's the test condition. Variant B isn't the "disciplined" choice — it's the control. You need both to have a comparison.

**4. Judge by process, not outcome.** In Stage 1, the current decision is correct if it was logged correctly — regardless of whether the trade won or lost. A logged early entry that loses is more valuable than an unlogged early entry that wins. The win teaches you nothing about the comparison. The logged entry, even losing, advances the experiment.

## LAYER 2 — The Mechanism

### From Guilt to Data

The psychological shift of Stage 1 is not subtle. When a behaviour moves from "my failure" to "my experiment," the emotional experience of executing it changes.

The trader who enters early and logs it as Variant A is not repeating a mistake. He's running a trial. The guilt that normally follows — the "I did it again" loop — has no foundation to attach to, because the behaviour was pre-authorised as part of the experiment. This is not permission to be undisciplined. It's a structural reframing: the early entry is not evidence of weakness, it's a data point in a question that will be answered.

This reframing reduces the emotional cost of the "wrong" variant significantly — which, counter-intuitively, makes it easier to execute the full-setup variant when the data eventually shows it's superior. Because the transition from A to B doesn't carry the weight of "I was always doing it wrong." It carries the weight of "the experiment is complete and the data showed a winner."

---

### Transgression — Repression Without Data

A trader tells herself she's going to wait for the full setup. She means it. For two sessions, she waits. On the third session, she sees the move developing before her conditions are met and enters early. She exits early too, anxious that the early entry might reverse, and misses most of the move.

She logs this as a failure. She recommits to the rule: wait for the full setup.

She has no data on whether her early entries win or lose more than her full-setup entries. She has only the guilt of the rule violation and the general principle that she "should" wait. Under urgency, the principle is no match for the pull. The guilt accumulates. The willpower depletes faster each session.

---

### Observance — The Experiment Runs

Same trader, different frame. She defines the experiment before the session: early entry = Variant A; full conditions = Variant B. Both are authorised. Both are to be logged. Both are data.

The next time she sees the move developing, she enters early. She logs it immediately: date, variant, conditions met (partial), entry, stop, target. She feels no guilt. The experiment is running.

Over the next four weeks, she logs 32 instances: 14 Variant A, 18 Variant B. She opens her log. Variant A: 36% win rate, average R:R 0.8. Variant B: 63% win rate, average R:R 1.4.

The experiment is complete. She now knows — not in principle, in data — that early entries cost her roughly 30 percentage points of win rate and 0.6R of expectancy per trade. She can calculate what that costs over a year.

The next time a setup develops and she feels the FOMO pull, the urgency is competing with two numbers she counted herself. Not a rule. Not a principle. A number. The pull is still there — but it's substantially weaker, because it's no longer pointing at an unanswered question.

## LAYER 3 — The Architecture

### Why Stage 1 Works Before the Data Arrives

The psychological benefit of Stage 1 doesn't wait for the data. It begins the moment the experiment is defined.

The mechanism: the vague doubt that generates urgency is replaced, structurally, by a concrete question that has a defined resolution path. The mind, given a concrete question and a process for answering it, stops running the open-loop search (see II.4). The search doesn't need to run anymore — it has an answer incoming.

This is why Stage 1 removes regret even before a single trade is logged. The "should I have done the other thing?" loop cannot run when *the other thing is also being measured*. The experiment structure makes the question answerable. Answerable questions don't generate the same urgency as unanswerable ones.

Stage 1 is the structural minimum — the smallest intervention that produces the largest immediate benefit. The data is months away. The regret dissolves the day you commit to collecting it.

---

### The Minimum Viable Experiment

The simplest Stage 1 experiment has three components:

1. **One concrete question.** Not multiple questions — one. The most expensive doubt you currently carry.

2. **Two clearly labelled alternatives.** A and B. No judgment in the labels — both are valid test conditions.

3. **A commitment to log both.** Not just the "right" one. Both. For as many instances as the setup occurs.

That's it. The analysis comes later. For now, the experiment just needs to run.
