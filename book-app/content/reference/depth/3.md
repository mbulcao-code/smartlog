# DEPTH DIVE 3 — The Science of Probabilities

### What a 65% Win Rate Actually Means — And What It Doesn't

---

A setup with a 65% historical win rate will, over any given sequence of trades, produce outcomes that look nothing like "65 wins in every 100 trades." Wins and losses are not evenly distributed. They cluster. They streak. They produce sequences that feel like patterns and are, probabilistically, expected noise.

Understanding this precisely — not as a vague reassurance, but as calculable mathematics — is what separates sample-thinking from event-thinking.

---

**The binomial distribution:**

If each trade on a 65%-win-rate setup is independent (the outcome of one trade doesn't affect the probability of the next), the distribution of outcomes over any sequence follows the binomial distribution. Some facts from that distribution that traders routinely find surprising:

On a 65% win-rate setup over 20 trades:
- The probability of a 3-trade losing streak: approximately 4.3%. Uncommon — but expected across a meaningful sample.
- The probability of a 4-trade losing streak: approximately 1.5%. Rare, but present in every long enough run.
- The probability of a 5-trade losing streak: approximately 0.5%. Very rare — but a trader running this setup for a year will likely see one.
- The probability of 5 consecutive losses at some point in 100 trades: approximately 40%.

The 5-consecutive-loss sequence that prompted the trader to conclude "something has changed about my edge" has a 40% probability of occurring in any 100-trade sample of an otherwise healthy setup. It is evidence of variance, not of a changed edge.

---

**The problem with event-thinking:**

Event-thinking evaluates each trade as an independent data point for a conclusion: "this trade lost — what went wrong?" When applied to a single loss in a series, this reasoning is appropriate: was the setup fully qualified, was the stop placed correctly, was the execution clean?

When event-thinking is applied to a losing streak — treating the sequence as a signal rather than the individual executions — it produces phantom conclusions. The "bad patch" narrative. The "I've lost my feel for the market" assessment. The recalibration of risk management downward precisely when the next clean trade is statistically likely.

Sample-thinking evaluates the sequence against the distribution: "is this sequence consistent with a 65% win-rate setup running normally, or is this streak in the tail that would indicate the distribution has changed?" These are different questions. The second is answerable with statistics. The first generates the self-blame loop.

---

**What a changed edge actually looks like:**

A genuine deterioration in setup performance is visible as a persistent shift in the win rate over a large sample — not a streak within a stable distribution. The signal: win rate over the last 30 trades drops below the lower bound of the confidence interval around the historical baseline.

Practical threshold: if the historical win rate is 65% and the last 30 trades show 43% or lower, the possibility of a genuine edge change is worth investigating (this is roughly a 2-standard-deviation event). If it shows 55%, that is within expected variance for 30 trials at 65% — investigation is not yet indicated.

The setup review — not a session review after a 3-trade losing streak, but a 30-trade performance review — is the instrument that detects genuine signal within the noise.

---

**Expected value and R:R:**

Expected value (EV) is the mathematical product of win rate and average win, minus the product of loss rate and average loss — stated in R terms:

EV = (win rate × average win in R) − (loss rate × average loss in R)

A setup with 65% win rate, 1.5R average win, 1R average loss:
EV = (0.65 × 1.5) − (0.35 × 1.0) = 0.975 − 0.35 = 0.625R per trade

This means: over a sufficient sample, this setup is expected to produce 0.625R per trade executed. A losing trade on this setup is not a failure — it is one of the 35% expected outcomes contributing to a positive expected-value process.

A setup with 40% win rate, 3R average win, 1R average loss:
EV = (0.40 × 3.0) − (0.60 × 1.0) = 1.2 − 0.6 = 0.6R per trade

This setup loses more than half its trades and has nearly identical positive EV. Event-thinking calls it a failing setup. EV thinking calls it a specific type of working one.

---

**Why this matters for the self-blame loop:**

The self-blame loop is fuelled primarily by event-thinking applied to loss sequences. When three consecutive losses feel like a pattern, they generate the Critic's case: "something has gone wrong." Sample-thinking looks at three consecutive losses and asks: "what is the probability of this sequence on my setup's known distribution?" The answer is almost always "expected." The case never gets built.

The reference distribution — the logged baseline — is the instrument that converts the streak from a qualitative feeling ("something's wrong") into a quantitative check ("is this within the expected range?"). Without the baseline, there's no check. With it, the self-blame loop's primary fuel is cut off.

---

**The research:**

The binomial distribution is standard probability theory. The specific application to trading performance evaluation draws on Taleb's *Fooled by Randomness* (2001) for the distinction between skill and variance in financial outcomes, and Mlodinow's *The Drunkard's Walk* (2008) for the general inaccessibility of randomness to human intuition. Kahneman and Tversky's work on the representativeness heuristic (1974) explains specifically why short sequences feel like representative samples — the mechanism behind streak-based conclusions.
