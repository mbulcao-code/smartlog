## GROUP 1 — Core Framework

---

### Competing Alternatives
Any situation where two or more legitimate goals make conflicting claims on the same decision. Competing alternatives are not a sign of psychological malfunction — they are structurally inevitable in trading because the Primary Goal and the Hidden Underlying Goal are both real. The question is not whether competition exists, but which type it is and how to design for it.
→ *[Act III.4: Three Types of Competing Alternatives]*

---

### Emotionally Stable Strategy (ESS)
A trading strategy designed to hold under emotional pressure by accounting for all active goals — not by suppressing the competing ones. An ESS has three properties: (1) all goals are acknowledged and given a channel, (2) the Primary Goal holds the healthy majority of the strategy, (3) every decision points toward resolution rather than accumulation of frustration. An ESS is not a perfect strategy. It is the most sustainable one available given the actual psychological conditions in which it must be executed.
→ *[Act IV.2: The ESS — structure and practical designs]*

---

### Hidden Underlying Goal (HUG)
The real goal a trading impulse is protecting — distinct from the stated Primary Goal and not visible on the surface of the behaviour. The HUG is not irrational. It is a legitimate goal (safety, agency, belonging, validation) that has recruited an impulsive behaviour as its delivery mechanism because no other mechanism has been designed. Identifying the HUG is the first step in building an ESS, because the ESS must satisfy it rather than suppress it.

Examples: the FOMO impulse protects the HUG of *guaranteed participation*; the early exit impulse protects the HUG of *terminating discomfort*; the revenge trade protects the HUG of *restored agency*.
→ *[Act I.2: Why the Loop is Inevitable — HUG in detail]*
→ *[Act II.2: All Habits Are Successful — how the HUG sustains the behaviour]*

---

### Mental Congress
The internal parliament of competing voices that governs trading decisions under pressure. The congress has three structural members plus one chair:

- **The Strategist** (chair): represents the Primary Goal; prioritises it without letting the HUG go unmet; issues the pre-trade thesis, manages the pirate fees, and chairs the post-session review
- **The Protector**: represents safety and risk management; wants to exit before the situation worsens; files the stop-tampering and early-exit motions
- **The Speculator**: represents opportunity and upside extraction; wants more exposure, more size, more continuation; files the FOMO, overtrading, and greed motions
- **The Critic**: represents identity protection; evaluates performance against a self-image standard; files the self-blame verdicts and generates hesitation after losses

The congress doesn't need to be silenced. It needs a chair who can negotiate, prioritise, and pre-commit decisions before the pirate speeches begin.
→ *[Act II.3: The Mental Congress — full structure and negotiation mechanics]*

---

### Pirate Fee
A pre-agreed, bounded concession to a competing goal — specifically to one of the three pirate voices — negotiated before the trade begins. The pirate fee is not a capitulation. It is the Strategist's tool for buying the pirates' cooperation rather than fighting their interference. A fee is defined by three properties: it is small (bounded in size), pre-agreed (not negotiated mid-trade under emotional activation), and non-expandable (the pirates don't renegotiate mid-trade).

Example: a 25% position at early-entry price for the FOMO Pirate, while the remaining 75% waits for the structural level. The FOMO Pirate is on salary. The fishermen keep their catch.
→ *[Act II.5: The Pirates' Dilemma — fee structures for all three pirates]*

---

### Primary Goal (PG)
The stated trading objective — the goal the trader would articulate if asked what they are trying to accomplish. In most trading contexts: profitable execution of a defined setup, with appropriate risk management, over a sufficient sample. The PG is the Strategist's mandate. An ESS prioritises the PG while providing channels for the HUG — it does not treat the two as equivalent.

---

### Silent Bomb
The accumulation of unacknowledged HUG-frustration over time. When a competing goal is repeatedly suppressed without being met, the frustration doesn't dissipate — it accumulates below the level of conscious awareness. When the accumulated pressure crosses the urgency threshold, the impulse fires without apparent cause ("I don't know why I snapped"). The silent bomb is why the loop worsens with repetition: each failed suppression adds pressure. The detonation always looks sudden; the accumulation was continuous.
→ *[Act I.2: Why the Loop is Inevitable]*

---

### Three Pirates
The three voices in the Mental Congress that compete with the Strategist's Primary Goal orientation:

1. **The FOMO Pirate** — wants guaranteed participation; speaks when price moves without the trader positioned; argues for early entry
2. **The Protector Pirate** — wants safety before loss occurs; speaks when the position is in drawdown or at risk of reversing a gain; argues for early exit, stop-moving, reduced size
3. **The Speculator Pirate** — wants maximum extraction; speaks when the trade is working or when recovery is needed; argues for holding past target, oversizing, overtrading

All three have legitimate goals. None should be silenced. All should be on salary before the trade begins.
→ *[Act II.5: The Pirates' Dilemma]*

## GROUP 2 — The Method

---

### Checklist (Sequential)
A pre-trade verification procedure in which conditions are evaluated independently, in a fixed sequence, one at a time, with a binary YES/NO and one sentence of written rationale recorded before the next condition is evaluated. Sequential evaluation prevents confirmation bias by making it structurally impossible to rationalise one condition against another simultaneously. A single NO prevents the entry — regardless of how strong the remaining conditions appear. See also: *Conditions*, *Variants*.
→ *[Pattern 11: Confirmation Bias — why sequence is the structural defence]*

---

### Comfort Stop
A stop level set where the loss feels emotionally acceptable to the trader. Distinct from the structural stop, which is set where market structure proves the thesis wrong. When the two diverge, the structural stop is always correct — the discomfort is addressed by reducing position size, not by widening the stop to match the comfort level. Comfort stops are the primary vehicle for loss aversion infiltrating risk management.
→ *[Pattern 5: Stop Loss Tampering]*

---

### Condition
An observable, pre-defined criterion that must be met for a setup to be valid. Conditions are not binary (yes/no on presence) — each condition has multiple valid alternatives called variants. A condition has two components: the category being assessed (e.g., momentum, structure, entry trigger) and the variants for how that category can be validly expressed. The setup is valid when all conditions are met; which variant of each condition is present becomes the data point for variant testing.
→ *[Act III.5: How to Build a Repeatable Setup]*
→ *[Act III.6: Conditions, Variants, and Why Both Matter]*

---

### Condition Audit
A post-session review of checklist compliance on a recent set of trades, typically triggered after a win streak or a period of suspected criteria loosening. Each trade is assessed for whether all conditions were genuinely fully met at the time of entry, or whether one or more were rated "borderline" or "close enough." The audit separates performance attributable to the defined setup from performance attributable to variance on relaxed criteria. A degraded compliance rate during a win streak is the early signal of overconfidence entering through selection quality.
→ *[Pattern 9: Overconfidence Bias]*

---

### Conviction Loop
The iterative process by which Stage 1 (Remove Regret) transitions into Stage 2 (Remove Uncertainty) and produces durable conviction. The loop: Execute the decision as a variant experiment → Log the outcome → Compare across the sample → Conclude from the data → Execute the next iteration with data-backed conviction → Refine → repeat. Conviction produced by this loop is statistical, not emotional — it survives the market's non-cooperation better than conviction produced by optimism or recent wins.
→ *[Act III.8: Reports: From Data to Confidence]*
→ *[Act III.3: Stage 2 — Remove Uncertainty]*

---

### Daily Loss Limit
A pre-defined maximum loss per session, stated in R (units of risk) rather than absolute dollar amounts. The daily loss limit is the structural defence against the redemption trap and revenge trading sequences: when the limit is reached, the platform closes — no exceptions, no redemption trades. Defined before the session begins; the decision is already made when the limit is hit. Not a concession to fear — a pre-commitment to rational session management that breaks the compounding mechanism.
→ *[Pattern 8: Redemption Trap]*
→ *[Act IV.3: Discipline Redefined — pre-commitment as structural shift #1]*

---

### Pre-Trade Thesis
A written prediction of what price is expected to do in the next session, and under what conditions the trader would trade it — committed before the market opens. The pre-trade thesis converts post-session chart review from retrospective pattern recognition (hindsight study) into predictive practice (real-time accuracy measurement). After the session, the thesis is evaluated not for whether it produced profit, but for whether the directional prediction was accurate. This distinction — prediction accuracy vs. trade win rate — is the training target that hindsight-based study cannot develop.
→ *[Pattern 12: Hindsight Bias — why the pre-trade thesis is the correction]*

---

### Repeatable Setup
A defined trading situation with four structural elements: (1) a Situation (the market context in which the setup is valid), (2) Conditions (each with variants), (3) a Structural Stop (the price that proves the thesis wrong), and (4) Targets (likelihood-based, with runner allocation if designed). A repeatable setup is the unit of analysis for the conviction loop — each instance generates logged data that feeds the Stage 2 comparison. Without a repeatable setup, there is no consistent sample to analyse and no baseline from which variants can be tested.
→ *[Act III.5: How to Build a Repeatable Setup]*

---

### R (Unit of Risk)
The amount risked on a single trade, defined as the distance between entry price and structural stop times position size. All P&L is expressed in R terms (e.g., 2.3R, -1R) to enable comparison across different instruments, position sizes, and account sizes. R-normalisation is what makes the conviction loop's comparison valid — comparing nominal dollar P&L across different conditions conflates setup performance with sizing decisions.

---

### Runner
The portion of a position (typically 20–25%) designated as the Speculator Pirate's fee — the allocation that continues past the primary target, with a pre-defined trailing stop rule. The runner is the structural solution to the greed pattern: the primary target is taken without negotiation; the runner gives the Speculator a bounded channel for capturing extended moves. The runner's trailing stop rule must be defined before the trade opens, not improvised during it.
→ *[Pattern 7: Greed / Holding Too Long]*
→ *[Act II.5: The Pirates' Dilemma — runner design]*

---

### Session Budget
A pre-defined constraint on the number of trades or total P&L loss per session. The session budget prevents overtrading by establishing the conditions under which the session closes — independent of what the Speculator argues mid-session. Typical forms: a maximum trade count (e.g., 5 trades per session), a daily loss limit in R, or a profit target at which optional closing is considered. The budget is set before the session; it does not require in-session willpower to enforce.
→ *[Pattern 6: Overtrading]*

---

### Speculative Allocation
A pre-authorised portion of session trading capacity reserved for off-plan trades — the FOMO Pirate's fee in a session-level budget. Typically: one off-plan trade per session at a maximum of 0.5R risk. The speculative allocation is not a permission to overtrade — it is a defined channel for the Speculator that prevents the channel from becoming unlimited. All speculative trades are logged separately from plan trades.
→ *[Pattern 6: Overtrading]*

---

### Stage 1 — Remove Regret
The first of two structured stages for resolving trading decisions under uncertainty. Stage 1 converts a vague doubt into a concrete, testable question. Four steps: (1) name the uncertainty precisely ("does variant A outperform variant B?"), (2) name the alternatives (define both variants), (3) test rather than repress (log both variants as they occur), (4) judge by process not outcome (a lost trade on a well-defined process is valid data). Stage 1 does not produce conviction — it produces the data from which conviction can be built. Completion of Stage 1 eliminates the regret loop ("I should have done the other thing") because both alternatives are being tested.
→ *[Act III.2: Stage 1 — Remove Regret]*

---

### Stage 2 — Remove Uncertainty
The second of two structured stages for resolving trading decisions under uncertainty. Stage 2 activates when the Stage 1 sample reaches a meaningful size (typically 20–30 instances per variant). At that point, the comparison answers the original question with statistical weight from the trader's own data. Uncertainty dissolves when the question it answered is closed. The urgency that was feeding the competing impulse loses its justification — not because the trader became stronger, but because the doubt that generated the urgency is gone.
→ *[Act III.3: Stage 2 — Remove Uncertainty]*

---

### Structural Stop
A stop level placed at the price that structurally invalidates the trade's thesis — the point at which the market has definitively answered "this read was wrong." Distinct from a comfort stop (which is placed where the loss feels acceptable). The structural stop is determined by market structure before entry, not adjusted based on how large the loss feels as price approaches it. When the structural stop and the comfort stop diverge, the correct fix is to reduce position size — not to move the stop to the comfort level.
→ *[Pattern 5: Stop Loss Tampering]*
→ *[Pattern 10: Loss Aversion]*

---

### The 3 Tasks
The three components of a complete trading approach, each of which must be addressed before Stage 2 conviction is possible:

1. **WHAT** — clear, specific goals. Not "be consistent" but "execute this defined setup with these specific conditions and this specific R:R target."
2. **HOW** — tested methods. A repeatable setup with logged performance history — not a loosely defined approach that hasn't been measured.
3. **WHAT TO EXPECT** — realistic expectations. A statistical baseline for win rate, average R:R, and expected drawdown sequences — built from the trader's own data, not theoretical projections.

Without all three, urgency has room to operate: the WHAT question leaves the Speculator arguing; the HOW question leaves the Protector uncertain; the WHAT TO EXPECT question turns expected variance into apparent evidence of failure.
→ *[Act II.4: The 3 Tasks]*

---

### Variant
One of the competing valid alternatives for a specific condition in a repeatable setup. Variants are not binary (present/absent) — they represent different ways the same condition can be validly expressed. Example: for the condition "entry trigger," Variant A might be a breakout candle, Variant B a reversal pattern, Variant C a failed test. Each variant is tracked separately in the log. Comparing variant performance over a meaningful sample is Stage 1 of the conviction loop — it converts the "which entry is better?" uncertainty into a counted comparison rather than an ongoing internal argument.
→ *[Act III.6: Conditions, Variants, and Why Both Matter]*

## GROUP 3 — The Loop

---

### Fighter Identity
The operational identity that treats emotion as the enemy of trading and willpower as the solution. The Fighter's diagnostic question after a loss: "How do I get stronger?" This question reinforces the loop it is trying to break, because it accepts the model (discipline = solution) and blames the executor when the model fails. The Fighter's loop: impulse fires → fight it → temporary success → fatigue → impulse returns → self-blame → scarred ego → loop intensifies. See also: *Strategist Identity*.
→ *[Act IV.1: From Fighter to Governor — the identity shift]*

---

### Self-Blame Loop
The iterative pattern in which failed impulse suppression produces a character verdict ("I'm weak / broken / not cut out for this"), which then becomes fuel for the next iteration of the same loop. The self-blame loop worsens with repetition because: (1) unmet HUG frustration accumulates, raising baseline urgency; (2) the Critic's material (self-supplied evidence of inadequacy) grows; (3) the verdict loads identity stakes onto every subsequent decision, which generates more activation, which generates faster impulses. The self-blame loop is self-sealing because self-blame protects the model: it makes questioning the model feel like weakness rather than accuracy — so the model that generates the loop never gets revised.
→ *[ENTRY.3: The Self-Blame Loop]*
→ *[Act I.3: Why Willpower Always Fails]*

---

### Strategist Identity (Governor)
The operational identity that treats emotion as signal and structure as the solution. The Strategist's diagnostic question after a loss: "What goal was the impulse protecting — and is there a strategy that satisfies both that goal and my PG?" This question leads to ESS design rather than increased willpower. The Strategist's loop: identify the HUG → design the ESS → pre-commit the fee → log the variant → compare the data → refine → execute with conviction. See also: *Fighter Identity*.
→ *[Act IV.1: From Fighter to Governor]*
→ *[Act IV.3: Discipline Redefined]*

---

### The Verdict
The character conclusion produced by the self-blame loop after repeated failed suppression attempts. Typically expressed as: "I'm not cut out for this," "I know what to do and can't make myself do it — what's wrong with me?", "other traders have what it takes; I don't." The verdict is not a logical conclusion — it is evidence of misdiagnosis. It is produced by applying the right effort (discipline) to the wrong problem (the symptom, not the source) for long enough that the only remaining explanation within the model is personal inadequacy. The verdict is expensive not just because it feels bad, but because it structurally accelerates the loop by raising the identity stakes of every subsequent trade.
→ *[ENTRY.4: The Verdict]*
→ *[Act I.4: The New Paradigm]*

## GROUP 4 — Competing Alternatives

---

### Hierarchic Goals
The second of three types of competing alternatives. Hierarchic goals are not in zero-sum competition — both can be satisfied simultaneously, proportionally. One goal is primary (takes the majority share); the other is secondary (given a defined allocation). The pirate fee design is the ESS implementation for hierarchic goals: the Strategist's PG takes the primary allocation; the pirate's HUG gets a bounded secondary allocation. Misdiagnosing hierarchic goals as zero-sum is the error that generates the suppression strategy — the trader tries to eliminate the competing goal rather than design for it proportionally.
→ *[Act III.4: Three Types of Competing Alternatives]*

---

### Pirates' Dilemma
The third of three types of competing alternatives — and the specific type that governs most trading impulses. The Pirates' Dilemma is a repeated-game competition where the two goals must coexist indefinitely. Unlike zero-sum competition, there is no final winner. Unlike hierarchic goals, the competition recurs on every trade. The ESS for the Pirates' Dilemma is the most sustainable ratio between the competing goals — not the maximum ratio for the Primary Goal. The pirate fee is the implementation: a recurring, pre-negotiated, bounded concession that keeps the pirate on salary rather than extracting at will.
→ *[Act II.5: The Pirates' Dilemma]*
→ *[Act III.4: Three Types of Competing Alternatives]*

---

### Zero-Sum Competition
The first of three types of competing alternatives. Zero-sum competition has one winner — one goal is satisfied, the other is not. True zero-sum situations require identifying which goal takes priority and executing accordingly. In trading, most apparently zero-sum situations (e.g., "should I enter early or wait?") are actually better designed as hierarchic or Pirates' Dilemma problems — but misdiagnosis in both directions is common and produces the wrong ESS structure.
→ *[Act III.4: Three Types of Competing Alternatives]*

## GROUP 5 — Cognitive Mechanisms

---

### Anchoring Bias
The tendency to weight an initial data point (an entry price, a recent high or low, a round number) disproportionately in subsequent evaluations. In trading, anchoring produces target adjustments ("I can't exit below my entry price"), stop rationalisation ("it's only a real loss when it passes this level"), and size decisions that reference the current account value relative to a prior high as though the prior high is the correct baseline. The fix is evaluating each decision against market structure and forward-looking probability, not against reference points from the trader's own account history.
→ *[Pattern 13: Anchoring / Recency Bias]*

---

### Confirmation Bias
The tendency to evaluate evidence in a way that confirms a conclusion already reached — rather than reaching a conclusion from the evidence. In trading, confirmation bias typically runs in the sequence: directional conviction forms (often from external social signals or intuition) → checklist is run in support of the conviction rather than as independent evaluation → conditions that don't align are rated "inconclusive" and passed → entry is taken on a case built to confirm rather than to verify. The structural defence is sequential, binary, written checklist evaluation — each condition assessed before the next, in a fixed order, independent of the directional conclusion.
→ *[Pattern 11: Confirmation Bias]*

---

### Hindsight Bias
The post-hoc feeling that a past price movement was predictable — and the associated error of conflating retrospective pattern recognition with real-time predictive ability. Hindsight bias is reinforced by closed-chart study: reviewing completed charts builds the skill of identifying patterns that led to the known outcome. This skill does not transfer to real-time trading, which requires predictions before outcomes are known. The correction is the pre-trade thesis: predictions written before the session, evaluated for directional accuracy after — not for whether they produced profit.
→ *[Pattern 12: Hindsight Bias]*

---

### Hyperbolic Discounting
The cognitive bias in which immediate rewards are weighted disproportionately relative to their actual value, compared to delayed rewards of equivalent or greater value. In trading: the relief from an early exit (immediate, certain) is weighted more heavily than the additional R available at the original target (delayed, probabilistic). The brain's reward system processes certainty and immediacy with a multiplier that expected-value reasoning cannot overcome in real time. The structural defence is pre-commitment — moving the exit decision to before the trade, when the multiplier isn't active.
→ *[Pattern 3: Early Exit / Cutting Profits Short]*
→ *[Act IV.3: Discipline Redefined — why pre-commitment works]*

---

### Loss Aversion
The evolved tendency to weight potential losses more heavily than equivalent potential gains. Loss aversion is not a flaw — it is adaptive behaviour that protected survival-level resource security for hundreds of thousands of years. In trading, it runs in an environment it wasn't built for: a trade loss is not a survival threat, but the threat response fires at the same intensity. Loss aversion peaks as the stop level approaches (proximity amplification) and operates on a biased memory that recalls recoveries more vividly than continued losses (asymmetric memory). The structural defence is the pre-placed stop order and the post-stop log — the first prevents mid-trade renegotiation; the second corrects the biased memory over time.
→ *[Pattern 10: Loss Aversion]*
→ *[Pattern 5: Stop Loss Tampering]*

---

### Recency Bias
The tendency to weight recent data disproportionately in expectations and decisions — in particular, to treat recent win or loss streaks as evidence of a changed edge rather than as expected variance within a known distribution. Recency bias produces streak-driven sizing: the trader sizes up after wins (believing the edge has strengthened) and sizes down after losses (believing the edge has deteriorated). Both responses add variance without adding expectancy. The structural defence is a reference distribution built from a 30+ trade sample — the anchor that puts any 3–5 trade sequence into its correct statistical context.
→ *[Pattern 13: Anchoring / Recency Bias]*
→ *[Pattern 9: Overconfidence Bias]*

## GROUP 6 — Structural Concepts

---

### ESS Stability Test
A four-question diagnostic for whether a proposed strategy will hold under real conditions:
1. Does the strategy account for every voice in the congress — or does it require one to be silenced?
2. Does the strategy resolve the HUG — or does it suppress it and accumulate pressure?
3. Does the strategy produce clear evidence over time — or does it leave the uncertainty unresolved?
4. Is the strategy executable in real time by the actual trader in actual conditions — or only by the ideal trader in ideal conditions?

A strategy that passes all four is structurally stable. A strategy that fails any one will show the failure consistently, at the precise moments when conditions are most demanding.
→ *[Act IV.2: The ESS — the stability test in full]*

---

### Pre-Commitment
A structural technique for making a decision durable by completing it before the conditions that would undermine it are active. Pre-commitment works by raising the cost of deviation (a live stop order must be actively cancelled; a written target requires an active decision to override; a defined session budget requires a deliberate violation). The technique does not require more willpower in the moment — it requires one pre-session decision that structures the choice environment in advance. Named in the literature for the myth of Odysseus ordering himself tied to the mast before the sirens' range.
→ *[Act IV.3: Discipline Redefined — pre-commitment as structural shift #1]*

---

### Sample Size
The number of logged instances of a specific setup or variant required to draw meaningful conclusions about its performance. General guidance: 20 instances of each variant for a directional comparison (which variant outperforms); 30+ instances for a baseline win rate that is resilient to individual runs; 50+ instances for decisions about sizing changes. Below these thresholds, the data is directional but not conclusive — label it "early signal, keep logging." Running a 3-trade sample as though it is evidence of a changed edge is the structural root of both overconfidence bias and limiting beliefs.
→ *[Act III.3: Stage 2 — sample size guidance]*
→ *[FAQ: How many trades before I have enough data?]*

---

### Two-Stage Decision Structure
The complete arc from "I'm not sure which approach is right" to "I know from my own data which approach is right" — the resolution of any meaningful trading uncertainty. Stage 1 removes regret by converting the uncertainty into a testable experiment. Stage 2 removes uncertainty by closing the question with counted data. The two stages operate in sequence — Stage 2 cannot be rushed; it requires the Stage 1 data to exist first. Most trading impulses persist because the trader has skipped Stage 1 and is waiting for certainty without the experiment that generates it.
→ *[Act III.1: The Two Stages of Structured Decisions]*
