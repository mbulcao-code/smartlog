# DEPTH DIVE 1 — Systems 1 and 2

### Why Your Brain Wins Arguments Against You

---

In 2002, Daniel Kahneman received the Nobel Prize in Economics for work he'd done with Amos Tversky two decades earlier. The central finding: human decision-making runs on two parallel systems that are structurally different, frequently in conflict, and weighted in ways that consistently produce predictable deviations from rational choice.

He called them System 1 and System 2. The labels are deliberately bland — they describe architecture, not personality.

---

**System 1** is fast, automatic, associative, and runs constantly. It processes pattern, emotion, and context without deliberate effort. It is responsible for: recognising a face, feeling unease before you can articulate why, the impulse to exit a trade when price approaches the stop, and the urgency that fires when the market moves without you. System 1 doesn't deliberate. It produces outputs — feelings, impulses, assessments — and delivers them to consciousness as completed conclusions.

**System 2** is slow, deliberate, effortful, and resource-dependent. It is responsible for: calculating position size, evaluating whether all checklist conditions are met, comparing variant A to variant B across a logged sample, and deciding whether to override the impulse System 1 just delivered. System 2 requires attention. It depletes. It is not available at full capacity for the entire trading session.

---

**The asymmetry that matters:**

System 1 operates continuously and at full speed regardless of session length, emotional activation, or decision fatigue. System 2 depletes.

This creates a predictable performance curve: at session open, System 2 is at full capacity and chairs most decisions. As the session progresses — especially after losses, after multiple decisions, after emotionally activating sequences — System 2's capacity diminishes. System 1 fills the gap. The decisions that were previously evaluated carefully are now evaluated quickly, on pattern and emotion.

This is not willpower failure. It is a structural feature of dual-process cognition. System 2 was never designed to operate indefinitely under sustained activation. The afternoon session is physiologically different from the morning session — the same trader, the same charts, a different ratio of System 1 to System 2 influence.

---

**Why this explains the specific timing of trading failures:**

Most traders fail on decisions that arrive under three conditions: high emotional activation, time pressure, and late in a depleted session. These three conditions are precisely when System 1 operates at maximum relative advantage. The FOMO that fires when the market moves fast and the trader isn't positioned. The stop move that happens when price has been approaching the level for twenty minutes. The revenge trade opened immediately after the loss.

All three arrive under conditions where System 2 is least available. System 1 is not stronger in these moments. System 2 is weaker. The outcome is the same: the impulse wins.

---

**The design implication:**

The solution is not to strengthen System 2. The solution is to move the critical decision out of the domain where System 1 and System 2 are competing in real time.

Pre-commitment achieves this: the Strategist's System 2 makes the decision before the session — before activation, before depletion, before the impulse has anything to gain from speed. The pre-placed stop order, the written target, the pirate fee on the card — these are System 2 decisions that have already been executed. When System 1 generates the urgency mid-session, it encounters a completed decision rather than an open question. The difference in outcome is structural, not motivational.

The further implication: session length matters. After a certain number of decisions, System 2's capacity drops below a threshold that makes adequate trade evaluation possible. The session budget is not arbitrary caution — it is a structurally informed limit on the window of reliable decision quality.

---

**The research:**

Kahneman's *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) is the accessible synthesis. The original experimental work with Tversky produced the Prospect Theory papers (1979, 1992) and the heuristics and biases research (1974 onward). The depletion literature — demonstrating that System 2 resources diminish with use — draws on Baumeister's ego depletion work (1998) and its subsequent replications and refinements.

# DEPTH DIVE 2 — Hyperbolic Discounting

### Why Certainty Now Beats Probability Later — Every Time

---

In classical economics, rational agents discount future rewards at a constant exponential rate: a reward available in 11 days is worth slightly less than the same reward available in 10 days, and the ratio between them is the same as the ratio between day 1 and day 2. The discount is smooth, consistent, and time-independent.

This is not how human beings work.

---

**The hyperbolic shape:**

Human temporal discounting is hyperbolic, not exponential. The discount curve drops steeply in the near term and flattens out for distant future rewards. This produces a specific and well-documented preference reversal: people prefer a smaller reward sooner over a larger reward later — even when they would choose the opposite if both options were shifted identically into the future.

The classic demonstration: most people prefer £10 today over £11 tomorrow. But the same people prefer £11 in 31 days over £10 in 30 days. The interval is identical. The absolute amounts are identical. The preference reverses because the first choice involves an immediate reward, and the hyperbolic curve applies a disproportionate discount to the one-day delay in the near-term version.

This is not irrationality in the pejorative sense. It is the output of a reward system calibrated by evolutionary pressure in environments where tomorrow was genuinely uncertain — where a guaranteed resource now was often more valuable than a probable resource later. The discounting is adaptive. It runs in an environment it was not designed for.

---

**What this means in a trade:**

The early exit delivers: relief from the discomfort of holding an open position in profit (immediate), and the lock-in of a smaller gain (certain). The original target delivers: a larger gain (delayed by however many candles remain), with the possibility of reversal (uncertain). The brain's reward system applies the hyperbolic multiplier to the early exit — not because the expected value calculation says to, but because immediate certainty is processed at a fundamentally different weight than delayed probability.

The Protector's speech feels rational because the relief it promises is genuinely more salient than the foregone target. The target is abstract. The relief is present and immediate. System 1 can experience the relief before the decision is made; it cannot experience the target gain in the same way. The asymmetry in experiential vividness compounds the hyperbolic discount.

---

**The compounding factor — loss framing:**

Prospect Theory (Kahneman and Tversky, 1979) adds a second mechanism: losses are experienced approximately twice as intensely as equivalent gains. A trade in profit that could give back gains is not evaluated as "a chance to gain more vs. a chance to gain less." It is evaluated — by the same system — as a chance to secure the gain vs. a chance to lose the gain already on the table. The potential reversal is framed as loss. The hyperbolic discount on the immediate exit and the loss-aversion multiplier on the potential reversal work in the same direction simultaneously.

Under these combined pressures, the early exit isn't a failure of discipline. It's the output of two well-documented cognitive mechanisms both pointing the same way, against a single deliberate counter-signal (the original target). The deliberate counter-signal needs to be pre-loaded into the architecture before the mechanisms activate.

---

**The pre-commitment solution, mechanically:**

Pre-commitment works because it converts a future choice into a past commitment. When the stop is placed as a live order before the trade opens, the Strategist's deliberate counter-signal is already executed. The hyperbolic discount and the loss-aversion multiplier activate during the trade — but they're now competing against a completed decision rather than an open one. Cancelling the order is a second deliberate act; the momentum of inaction works in the Strategist's favour rather than against it.

The partial exit design addresses hyperbolic discounting without fighting it: the Protector gets the certain, immediate partial gain (50-60% of the position at a defined interim level). The hyperbolic urgency is satisfied at bounded cost. The remainder runs under a pre-defined structure. The urgency and the target coexist because one is on salary.

---

**The research:**

Ainslie (1975) was the first formal treatment of hyperbolic discounting in humans and other animals. Loewenstein and Prelec (1992) developed the mathematical characterisation. Thaler (1981) documented the preference reversal pattern that the hyperbolic model explains. The behavioural economics literature from Thaler and Sunstein's *Nudge* (2008) draws extensively on this mechanism for policy design — the pre-commitment insight generalises beyond trading directly from their work.

# DEPTH DIVE 3 — The Science of Probabilities

### What a 65% Win Rate Actually Means — And What It Doesn't

---

A setup with a 65% historical win rate will, over any given sequence of trades, produce outcomes that look nothing like "65 wins in every 100 trades." Wins and losses are not evenly distributed. They cluster. They streak. They produce sequences that feel like patterns and are, probabilistically, expected noise.

Understanding this precisely — not as a vague reassurance, but as calculable mathematics — is what separates sample-thinking from event-thinking.

---

**The binomial distribution:**

If each trade on a 65%-win-rate setup is independent (the outcome of one trade doesn't affect the probability of the next), the distribution of outcomes over any sequence follows the binomial distribution. Some facts from that distribution that traders routinely find surprising:

On a 65% win-rate setup over 20 trades:
- The probability of a 3-trade losing streak: approximately 4.3%. Uncommon — but expected across a meaningful sample.
- The probability of a 4-trade losing streak: approximately 1.5%. Rare, but present in every long enough run.
- The probability of a 5-trade losing streak: approximately 0.5%. Very rare — but a trader running this setup for a year will likely see one.
- The probability of 5 consecutive losses at some point in 100 trades: approximately 40%.

The 5-consecutive-loss sequence that prompted the trader to conclude "something has changed about my edge" has a 40% probability of occurring in any 100-trade sample of an otherwise healthy setup. It is evidence of variance, not of a changed edge.

---

**The problem with event-thinking:**

Event-thinking evaluates each trade as an independent data point for a conclusion: "this trade lost — what went wrong?" When applied to a single loss in a series, this reasoning is appropriate: was the setup fully qualified, was the stop placed correctly, was the execution clean?

When event-thinking is applied to a losing streak — treating the sequence as a signal rather than the individual executions — it produces phantom conclusions. The "bad patch" narrative. The "I've lost my feel for the market" assessment. The recalibration of risk management downward precisely when the next clean trade is statistically likely.

Sample-thinking evaluates the sequence against the distribution: "is this sequence consistent with a 65% win-rate setup running normally, or is this streak in the tail that would indicate the distribution has changed?" These are different questions. The second is answerable with statistics. The first generates the self-blame loop.

---

**What a changed edge actually looks like:**

A genuine deterioration in setup performance is visible as a persistent shift in the win rate over a large sample — not a streak within a stable distribution. The signal: win rate over the last 30 trades drops below the lower bound of the confidence interval around the historical baseline.

Practical threshold: if the historical win rate is 65% and the last 30 trades show 43% or lower, the possibility of a genuine edge change is worth investigating (this is roughly a 2-standard-deviation event). If it shows 55%, that is within expected variance for 30 trials at 65% — investigation is not yet indicated.

The setup review — not a session review after a 3-trade losing streak, but a 30-trade performance review — is the instrument that detects genuine signal within the noise.

---

**Expected value and R:R:**

Expected value (EV) is the mathematical product of win rate and average win, minus the product of loss rate and average loss — stated in R terms:

EV = (win rate × average win in R) − (loss rate × average loss in R)

A setup with 65% win rate, 1.5R average win, 1R average loss:
EV = (0.65 × 1.5) − (0.35 × 1.0) = 0.975 − 0.35 = 0.625R per trade

This means: over a sufficient sample, this setup is expected to produce 0.625R per trade executed. A losing trade on this setup is not a failure — it is one of the 35% expected outcomes contributing to a positive expected-value process.

A setup with 40% win rate, 3R average win, 1R average loss:
EV = (0.40 × 3.0) − (0.60 × 1.0) = 1.2 − 0.6 = 0.6R per trade

This setup loses more than half its trades and has nearly identical positive EV. Event-thinking calls it a failing setup. EV thinking calls it a specific type of working one.

---

**Why this matters for the self-blame loop:**

The self-blame loop is fuelled primarily by event-thinking applied to loss sequences. When three consecutive losses feel like a pattern, they generate the Critic's case: "something has gone wrong." Sample-thinking looks at three consecutive losses and asks: "what is the probability of this sequence on my setup's known distribution?" The answer is almost always "expected." The case never gets built.

The reference distribution — the logged baseline — is the instrument that converts the streak from a qualitative feeling ("something's wrong") into a quantitative check ("is this within the expected range?"). Without the baseline, there's no check. With it, the self-blame loop's primary fuel is cut off.

---

**The research:**

The binomial distribution is standard probability theory. The specific application to trading performance evaluation draws on Taleb's *Fooled by Randomness* (2001) for the distinction between skill and variance in financial outcomes, and Mlodinow's *The Drunkard's Walk* (2008) for the general inaccessibility of randomness to human intuition. Kahneman and Tversky's work on the representativeness heuristic (1974) explains specifically why short sequences feel like representative samples — the mechanism behind streak-based conclusions.

# DEPTH DIVE 4 — Evolutionary Game Theory

### Why the Most Sustainable Strategy Beats the Locally Optimal One

---

In 1973, biologist John Maynard Smith introduced the concept of the Evolutionarily Stable Strategy (ESS) to explain a puzzle in evolutionary biology: why do animals within the same species often use mixed strategies in conflict situations, rather than universally adopting the most aggressive or most successful individual strategy?

The puzzle: if hawks (aggressive fighters) consistently beat doves (conflict-avoiders) in individual contests, why do dove-like behaviours persist in populations? And conversely, why don't pure hawk populations simply dominate?

The answer — one of the most elegant in all of evolutionary theory — reframes what "optimal" means.

---

**The Hawk-Dove Game:**

In Maynard Smith's model, two strategies compete for a resource:

- **Hawk:** always escalates to full conflict; takes the resource if the opponent backs down; risks serious injury if the opponent doesn't.
- **Dove:** displays, but retreats if the opponent escalates; avoids injury but often loses the resource.

When the population is all-Dove, a single Hawk mutant wins every contest — it always escalates, and Doves always retreat. The Hawk strategy spreads.

When the population is all-Hawk, every contest is a full fight. Serious injuries are common. The average payoff from fighting an opponent who never backs down is very low — sometimes negative (the injury cost exceeds the resource value). A Dove mutant in an all-Hawk population avoids injury in every contest — a low return, but a non-negative one. The Dove strategy can spread.

The stable outcome is neither all-Hawk nor all-Dove. It is a mixed equilibrium — either a population with a specific ratio of Hawks to Doves, or individual animals that mix strategies situationally. This mixture is the ESS: the strategy that, once established in a population, cannot be invaded by any other strategy because its average payoff exceeds any alternative.

---

**The key insight: stability is not the same as local optimality.**

In the all-Dove population, the Hawk strategy has the highest individual payoff. Locally, it's optimal. But as Hawks spread, they increasingly encounter each other — and the injury costs mount. The locally optimal strategy sows the conditions of its own decline.

The ESS is not the strategy with the highest individual payoff in any given context. It is the strategy that remains viable when it becomes common — when it has to compete against itself, not against a naive population.

---

**Why this maps to the trading mind:**

The three pirates (FOMO Pirate, Protector Pirate, Speculator Pirate) are not irrational. Each has a strategy that is locally optimal under specific conditions:

- The FOMO Pirate's strategy (enter early, guarantee participation) is locally optimal when the market is moving fast and misses are painful.
- The Protector's strategy (exit early, secure the gain) is locally optimal when a recent reversal has occurred or is feared.
- The Speculator's strategy (hold longer, size bigger) is locally optimal after a winning sequence when confidence is high.

Each strategy wins in its specific context. But a trading session run entirely by any one of them is not a session with the highest expected value. A session dominated by the FOMO Pirate produces compressed R:R on every entry. A session dominated by the Protector produces consistent early exits that leave the majority of available return on the table. A session dominated by the Speculator produces oversized positions on loosened criteria.

The ESS is the mixed strategy that survives when each voice gets a turn — the ratio at which the total payoff (across all sessions, all market conditions, all emotional states) is maximised. That ratio is not determined theoretically. It is discovered empirically, through the conviction loop: which mixture of pirate fees, structural entries, and pre-committed exits actually produces the best logged results over a meaningful sample?

---

**The Strategist's evolutionary function:**

Maynard Smith's framework offers a further insight: in mixed-strategy populations, there must be a mechanism that enforces the ratio. In biological populations, it's natural selection — strategies that overshoot the stable ratio experience declining fitness until they return to equilibrium. In the trading mind, the mechanism is the Strategist: the voice that enforces the agreed ratio by pre-committing the fees and maintaining the ESS under pressure.

Without the Strategist's enforcement, the loudest pirate takes the field, overshoots his optimal allocation, and diminishes the total return. With it, each pirate receives a bounded channel, the sum of their allocations is controlled, and the total remains above the threshold that keeps the trading enterprise viable.

This is why the Strategist's role is not to silence the pirates but to chair the congress. Silencing doesn't maintain a stable ratio — it suppresses one strategy until it erupts. Chairing negotiates the ratio and holds it.

---

**The research:**

Maynard Smith and Price (1973) introduced the ESS concept in a *Nature* paper on animal conflict. Maynard Smith's *Evolution and the Theory of Games* (1982) is the full treatment. Richard Dawkins' *The Selfish Gene* (1976) made the concept accessible to a general audience through the "Hawk-Dove" framing used here. The application of ESS thinking to human strategic behaviour draws on the broader field of evolutionary game theory developed by Axelrod (*The Evolution of Cooperation*, 1984) — whose iterated Prisoner's Dilemma tournaments independently confirmed that mixed, reciprocal strategies outperform locally dominant aggressive ones over repeated interactions.

# DEPTH DIVE 5 — The Hypothetico-Deductive Method

### Why the Variant Experiment Is the Scientific Method

---

The most successful knowledge-generating practice in human history is not intuition, authority, or tradition. It is a specific structure for turning uncertain beliefs into testable predictions and updating the beliefs based on whether the predictions hold.

That structure is the hypothetico-deductive method. It is also, structurally, exactly what the variant experiment does.

---

**The four steps:**

1. **Observation:** notice something that requires explanation. Price seems to behave differently when entry is taken at the structural level vs. above it.

2. **Hypothesis:** form a specific, falsifiable claim. "Structural-level entries (Variant B) outperform early entries (Variant A) in my primary setup, as measured by win rate and average R:R over 20+ instances."

3. **Prediction:** derive a testable consequence. "Over the next 25 instances of each variant, Variant B will show a higher win rate and average R:R than Variant A."

4. **Test:** observe the actual outcomes and compare to the prediction. The hypothesis is supported, refuted, or refined based on what the data shows.

The critical requirement — the one that makes this scientific rather than pseudo-scientific — is **falsifiability**: the hypothesis must be stated in a way that a specific result could prove it wrong. "Variant B might work better sometimes" is not a hypothesis. "Variant B outperforms Variant A by at least 10 percentage points in win rate across 25 instances" is.

---

**What makes a trading experiment valid:**

A valid variant experiment requires four properties:

**Pre-registration:** the variants must be defined before the data is collected, not after. Defining Variant A and Variant B after you've already logged 20 trades and can see which looks better is not hypothesis testing — it is pattern-fitting to noise. The same data that generated the hypothesis cannot be used to confirm it.

**Consistent criteria:** every instance of each variant must be selected by the same criteria. If Variant A includes "entered before the level" and Variant B includes "entered at the level or below," every logged instance must be cleanly categorisable into one or the other. Mixed or ambiguous instances should be logged as such and excluded from the comparison.

**Sufficient sample:** as detailed in the Probabilities Depth Dive, fewer than 20 instances per variant produces noise-dominated comparisons. The comparison is directional below 20, meaningful at 20–30, reliable above 30.

**Single variable:** the ideal variant test changes one thing at a time. "Variant A vs. Variant B" should differ on exactly one dimension (entry timing, entry trigger type, position size) — not two or three simultaneously. When multiple variables change between variants, it's impossible to attribute the performance difference to any specific one.

---

**Why this matters beyond the method:**

The hypothetico-deductive structure does something psychologically important that no amount of principle-based trading advice achieves: it converts the trader from a subject of the market's judgment into an investigator of a specific question. The market's verdict on any individual trade is no longer a judgment of the trader's competence — it is data for an ongoing investigation.

This reframe is not cosmetic. The Critic's primary argument is "the loss tells you something about you." The investigator's frame converts the same loss to "the loss tells me something about this variant's performance in this condition." The Critic loses its raw material. Not because the trader becomes indifferent to losses — but because the losses have been recruited to a different purpose.

The investigator identity — the trader who is running an experiment, collecting data, and will draw a conclusion from the results — is one of the few identity frames that makes the self-blame loop structurally impossible to run. You can't blame yourself for the results of a properly controlled experiment. The results are evidence. The evidence goes into the analysis. The analysis produces the next hypothesis.

---

**The limiting case — what trading can't test:**

Not everything a trader wants to know is testable by this method. Specifically: questions that require simultaneous live experience of both variants are not directly testable (you cannot hold a position and simultaneously test what would have happened if you hadn't). The counterfactual — "would I have made more if I'd held?" — is answered by tracking what price did after the early exit, not by experiencing both outcomes simultaneously.

For this reason, the log must capture post-exit price behaviour, not just trade outcomes at exit. The comparison between actual exit and what-would-have-happened-if-held is the closest approximation to a controlled experiment the live trading environment allows.

---

**The research:**

Karl Popper's *The Logic of Scientific Discovery* (1959) established falsifiability as the demarcation criterion for scientific claims. The hypothetico-deductive structure was formalised in the philosophy of science literature through Hempel and Oppenheim's work on the deductive-nomological model (1948). The specific application to belief updating in contexts of uncertainty draws on Bayesian reasoning — where prior beliefs are updated by likelihood ratios derived from evidence — making each variant comparison a formal update on a prior about which strategy is superior.

# DEPTH DIVE 6 — The Ulysses Contract

### Why Binding Future Decisions Works — And Why It Has to Be Done Right

---

In Homer's *Odyssey*, Odysseus (Ulysses in the Latin tradition) is warned that his ship will pass within range of the Sirens — creatures whose singing compels sailors to steer toward the rocks and drown. Odysseus wants to hear the Sirens' song without dying. His solution: he orders his crew to lash him to the mast and fill their own ears with wax. He instructs them explicitly — in advance, in cold blood — that when he begs and orders them to release him, they are to refuse. He pre-commits.

The crew executes the plan perfectly. Odysseus hears the song, begs for release with full conviction, and is denied. The ship passes safely. He got what he wanted (the experience of the song) without the consequence he couldn't afford (the wreck).

---

**Why the story is useful:**

Odysseus understood something that the standard model of self-control misses entirely: when the Sirens' song arrives, he will not be reasoning. He will be experiencing an overwhelming compulsion that will feel, in that moment, like genuine insight ("I should steer toward that sound"). The compulsion will produce arguments — good arguments, voiced with full conviction — for why the plan should change.

He knew this in advance. And rather than try to be stronger when the compulsion arrived, he removed the option. The crew's instructions converted "I am being urged to do something I already decided against" from an open question requiring real-time willpower into a closed question with a pre-executed answer.

The pre-commitment doesn't require Odysseus to resist the Sirens in the moment. It requires one act of Strategist-level decision-making — before the activation — that structures the moment of maximum urgency in advance.

---

**The psychological mechanism:**

Pre-commitment works through three distinct mechanisms:

**Raising the cost of deviation:** a live stop order requires active cancellation — a deliberate act against an already-executed decision. A written target on a card requires a conscious override. A daily loss limit that closes the platform requires a specific decision to violate it. None of these are impossible. But each adds friction between the urgency and the action. Under System 1 activation, friction is enormously effective: the default path (honouring the pre-commitment) becomes the path of least resistance.

**Externalising the decision:** when the stop is a live order rather than a mental note, the question "should I move the stop?" requires a different framing than "should I leave the stop alone?" The pre-committed position is the reference point. Deviation requires an active argument against a completed decision. This asymmetry consistently favours the pre-committed choice.

**Distributing decision authority across time:** the Ulysses Contract is fundamentally a conversation between the Strategist in cold blood (before the session) and the pirate under activation (during the trade). The pre-session Strategist has more information and better judgment. The in-trade congress is activated, partially depleted, and subject to recency and urgency bias. Pre-commitment is the mechanism that gives the more competent decision-maker's output priority — not by suppressing the pirates, but by making their preferred alternative structurally harder to execute.

---

**What makes a pre-commitment effective:**

Not all pre-commitments work equally. The research (and practical trading experience) suggests four properties of effective ones:

**Specificity:** "I will hold my stop" is less effective than "the stop is a live working order placed at [price] and will not be cancelled." The first is an intention. The second is a structure.

**External enforcement:** the crew following orders is more effective than Odysseus trying to remember his intention while hearing the song. External enforcement translates to trading as: live orders rather than mental notes; a written card rather than a remembered commitment; a platform rule rather than a personal guideline. The key principle: enforcement that doesn't rely on in-the-moment willpower to activate.

**Completeness:** partial pre-commitment creates partial protection. A pre-committed stop that has an exception clause ("unless the market structure clearly changes") provides a rationalisation vehicle for the Protector. The exception clause will be invoked at the worst moment. Pre-commitments without exception clauses are more effective — and the correct response to "but what if the thesis genuinely changes?" is a separately defined rule, not an in-trade exception.

**Proportionality:** the constraint should match the problem. Pre-committing the stop does nothing about the target exit. Pre-committing the target does nothing about the stop. Each pirate requires its own pre-commitment, sized to its specific interference point. A blanket "I will follow my plan" is not a pre-commitment — it's an aspiration.

---

**The pirate fee as a form of Ulysses Contract:**

The pirate fee is specifically designed to address the case where a full pre-commitment is neither possible nor desirable — where the competing goal is legitimate enough that total suppression produces worse outcomes than managed accommodation.

Odysseus could have simply plugged his own ears. He chose not to because he genuinely wanted the experience of the Sirens' song. The fee is the equivalent: he pre-commits to hearing the song (satisfying the competing goal) while pre-committing to the mast (preventing the destructive action). Both commitments are made in advance. Neither is left to in-the-moment negotiation.

For the FOMO Pirate: the fee is the 25% early entry. The mast is the remaining 75% waiting for the structural level. Both are pre-committed. The Pirate gets the song. The ship doesn't wreck.

---

**The research:**

The Ulysses Contract as a concept in behavioural economics was formalised by Elster (*Ulysses and the Sirens*, 1979). Thaler and Benartzi developed practical pre-commitment mechanisms in the retirement savings context (the Save More Tomorrow program, 2004) — one of the most effective applications of the principle in policy design. Ariely's *Predictably Irrational* (2008) documents pre-commitment in experimental settings. The clinical application of pre-commitment — patient-written directives about future medical treatment during competent periods, binding during incompetent ones — follows the identical structure and is well-established in bioethics.
